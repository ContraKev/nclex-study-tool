## 📊 CHAPTER 20: THE ADOLESCENT

### Physical Development
| Concept | Details |
|---------|---------|
| **Puberty** | Period of rapid reproductive maturation; begins with secondary sex characteristics, ends with mature sperm/regular menses |
| **Growth Spurt** | Final 20% of adult height; girls mature 6mo–2yr before boys |
| **Asynchrony** | Body parts mature at different rates → awkward, gangly appearance |
| **Tanner Stages** | Used to assess sexual maturity |

#### Boys (10-13yr onset)
- Voice deepens, hair growth (face, chest, axillae, pubic)
- Testicular enlargement → penile enlargement
- **Teach**: Monthly testicular self-exam

#### Girls
- Breast development, menarche (10-15yr)
- **Teach**: Monthly breast self-exam

#### Skin & Acne
- Active sweat glands → oily skin, acne common
- Teach gentle cleansing, avoid picking, OTC treatments

### Psychosocial Development
#### Erikson: Identity vs. Role Confusion
- Major task: Establish identity, separate from family, initiate intimacy
- **Emancipation**: Critical for developing individuality
- May reject childhood self and family; question parental values
- Disagreements common around: dating, car, money, grades, friends, smoking, sex, drugs

#### Piaget: Formal Operational Stage
- Abstract reasoning, hypothetical thinking
- Can understand symbols, religious concepts beyond family tradition

#### Freud: Genital Stage
- Focus on mature sexual relationships

### Key Developmental Tasks
- Establishing identity
- Separating from family
- Initiating intimacy
- Developing career choices for economic independence

### Common Adolescent Issues (Nursing Focus)
| Issue | Nursing Interventions |
|-------|----------------------|
| Body image disturbance | Reinforce healthy self-concept; educate on normal variations |
| Risk-taking behavior | Safety education (seat belts, helmet, STI prevention, substance use) |
| Peer pressure | Teach refusal skills, assertiveness |
| Eating disorders | Screen for anorexia/bulimia; assess body image, eating patterns |
| Depression/suicide | Screen; assess for hopelessness; refer immediately if suicidal ideation |

### Sexual Health
- STI prevention education (condoms, abstinence)
- Contraception counseling (confidentiality, parental notification laws vary)
- Pregnancy prevention

### Cultural/Spiritual Considerations
- Adolescents explore family values and may embrace or separate from them
- Exposure to other religions/practices helps stabilize group identity

## 🏥 CHAPTER 21: THE CHILD'S EXPERIENCE OF HOSPITALIZATION

### Health Care Delivery Settings
- Acute care hospital
- Extended/long-term care facility
- Urgent care center
- Outpatient clinic/office
- Pediatric research center
- Outpatient surgery center
- Home care

### Separation Anxiety (6 months+; most pronounced in toddlers)
| Stage | Behavior | Nursing Interventions |
|-------|----------|----------------------|
| **Protest** | Loud crying, calls for parent, watches/listens for them | Allow parent to stay; provide comfort items |
| **Despair** | Sad, depressed, withdrawn; less active play | Encourage parent presence; therapeutic play |
| **Denial/Detachment** | Appears to deny need for parent; detached, uninterested in visits | Prepare parents; encourage frequent visits |

### Age-Specific Reactions to Hospitalization

#### Infants
- Stranger anxiety peaks; maintain routines
- Prepare: Provide comfort objects, parental presence

#### Toddlers/Preschoolers
- Separation anxiety most intense
- Magical thinking → may believe illness is punishment
- Prepare: Use simple terms, allow choices, demonstrate equipment

#### School-Age Children
- Need control and understanding
- Allow to examine/play with equipment
- Prepare: Explain procedures honestly; offer rewards

#### Adolescents
- Privacy is critical
- Involve in decisions
- Prepare: Discuss coping techniques; provide privacy

### Preparation for Procedures (Box 21.1 pg. 480)
- Tailor to developmental stage
- Use simple, honest language
- Offer choices when possible
- Provide distraction (bubbles, toys, videos)
- Allow play with equipment before use
- Reward after procedure

### Therapeutic Play
- Reduces anxiety and fear
- Types: Medical play (doll with IV), expressive play (drawing feelings)
- Helps children process hospitalization experience

### Family-Centered Care
- Parents are partners in care
- Assess parental stress; provide support
- Educate parents on their role in child's care
- Allow parents to stay overnight when possible

## 🩺 CHAPTER 22: HEALTH CARE ADAPTATIONS FOR THE CHILD AND FAMILY

### Admission to Pediatric Unit — Nursing Responsibilities
- Obtain informed consent (parent/guardian; child assent if age-appropriate)
- Orient child and family to unit
- Establish rapport and trust

### Basic Data Collection
| Component | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Observation** | Level of interaction, behavior, affect |
| **Growth & Development** | Compare to age norms (height, weight, head circ) |
| **History Survey** | Chief complaint, past medical history, immunizations, family history |
| **Pain Assessment** | Use age-appropriate scale (FLACC, FACES, numeric) |

### Physical Survey
- Vital signs (age-specific normal ranges)
- Weight (infants in grams)
- Height
- Head circumference (infants until 2-3yr)
- Pain level

### Specimen Collection
1. Verify physician order
2. Obtain lab requisitions, correct containers, supplies
3. Collect specimen (age-appropriate technique)
4. Label clearly with patient ID
5. Send to lab per policy
6. Document in notes and I&O record

### Medication Administration in Infants & Children
| Factor | Consideration |
|--------|---------------|
| **Absorption** | Oral: Stomach pH higher in infants; IM: Smaller muscle mass |
| **Metabolism** | Liver immature; slower drug metabolism |
| **Excretion** | Kidneys immature; longer half-life |
| **Age** | MOST IMPORTANT variable in predicting drug response |

#### Nursing Responsibilities
- **ALWAYS** calculate safe dose before administration
- Observe for toxic symptoms
- Routes: Oral (use oral syringe, never household spoon), Parenteral (small needles), IV (microdrip, careful rate), TPN (total parenteral nutrition)

### Pain Management
- Use age-appropriate assessment tools
- Pharmacologic: Acetaminophen, ibuprofen, opioids (weight-based dosing)
- Non-pharmacologic: Distraction, positioning, comfort measures, parental presence

## 👂👁 CHAPTER 23: THE CHILD WITH A SENSORY OR NEUROLOGICAL CONDITION

### Ear Anatomy Differences (Newborn vs Adult)
| Feature | Newborn | Adult |
|---------|---------|-------|
| Tympanic membrane | Almost horizontal | Angled |
| Vascularity | More vascular | Less |
| Light reflex | Inconsistent | Clear |
| Eustachian tube | Shorter, straighter | Longer, angled |

#### Eustachian Tube Functions
- Ventilation (equalizes pressure)
- Protection (from nasopharyngeal secretions)
- Drainage (middle ear secretions)

### Otitis Externa (Swimmer's Ear)
- Acute infection of external canal
- **Signs**: Pain/tenderness on pinna/tragus manipulation; tympanic membrane normal
- **Treatment**: Irrigation + topical antibiotics/antivirals
- **Nursing**: Insert wick after drops if canal swollen

### Acute Otitis Media (AOM)
- Inflammation of middle ear
- **Cause**: Often follows URI; *S. pneumoniae*, *H. influenzae*
- **Risk**: Infants more prone due to anatomy; immature immunity
- **Signs**: Fever, irritability, pulling at ear, decreased hearing
- **Treatment**: Antibiotics (amoxicillin first-line), analgesics, decongestants

#### Nursing Care
- Instill ear drops correctly: **Infants** — pull pinna DOWN and BACK; **Children** — pull pinna UP and BACK
- Teach completion of full antibiotic course
- Follow-up in 48-72hr if not improving

### Hearing Impairment
- **Causes**: Congenital (TORCH infections, genetic), acquired (ototoxic drugs, meningitis, chronic OM)
- **Assessment**: Newborn hearing screening; observe for response to sound
- **Nursing**:
  - Face child when speaking; use visual cues
  - Speak clearly, normal volume
  - Use sign language/interpreter if appropriate
  - Encourage cochlear implant or hearing aids as indicated

### Eye Disorders

#### Strabismus (Crossed Eyes)
- Misalignment of eyes; constant or intermittent
- **Types**: Paralytic (nerve/muscle) vs Nonparalytic (refractive)
- **Treatment**: Patching good eye (to strengthen weak eye), corrective lenses, surgery if needed
- **Complication**: Amblyopia if untreated

#### Amblyopia (Lazy Eye)
- Reduced vision in one eye from disuse
- **Treatment**: Patch stronger eye to force use of weaker eye

#### Prevention of Eyestrain
- Proper lighting, reading distance
- Limit screen time
- Annual vision screening

### Seizure Disorders
- **Febrile seizures**: Most common in children 6mo-5yr; brief, generalized; usually benign
- **Epilepsy**: Recurrent unprovoked seizures
- **Nursing during seizure**:
  - Protect from injury (clear area, side-lying)
  - Time seizure; do NOT restrain
  - Do NOT put anything in mouth
  - Suction if needed; administer oxygen
  - Administer rescue meds (rectal diazepam) if ordered
  - Postictal: Position for airway; monitor vitals

## 🦴 CHAPTER 24: THE CHILD WITH A MUSCULOSKELETAL CONDITION

### Characteristics of Child's Musculoskeletal System
| Feature | Implication |
|---------|-------------|
| Bone not fully ossified | More porous; fractures heal faster |
| Epiphyses present | Growth plates; bone overgrowth common after fracture in <10yr |
| Periosteum thick | Produces callus rapidly; better healing |
| Lower mineral content | Bones stronger despite porosity |

### Musculoskeletal Assessment
- **Observation**: Gait, posture, muscle tone, symmetry
- **Palpation**: Tenderness, swelling, warmth
- **ROM**: Passive and active
- **Gait Assessment**:
  - Toddler: Wide, unstable gait; arms don't swing
  - By 18mo: Base narrows, more stable
  - By 4yr: Can hop on one foot
  - By 6yr: Adult-like gait
- ⚠️ Child not walking by 18mo → refer for evaluation

### Common Disorders

#### Fractures
- **Greenstick**: Incomplete fracture (one side bends, other breaks) — common in children
- **Buckle/torus**: Compression fracture
- **Healing**: Faster than adults due to active periosteum

#### Legg-Calvé-Perthes Disease
- Avascular necrosis of femoral head
- **Age**: 4-10yr; boys > girls
- **Signs**: Limp, hip pain, limited ROM
- **Treatment**: Rest, bracing, surgery if severe

#### Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis (SCFE)
- Femoral head slips at growth plate
- **Age**: 10-16yr; overweight adolescents at higher risk
- **Signs**: Hip/knee pain, limp, external rotation of leg
- **Treatment**: Surgical pinning

#### Developmental Dysplasia of the Hip (DDH)
- Abnormal hip development
- **Assessment**: Ortolani/Barlow maneuvers in infants
- **Treatment**: Pavlik harness (newborns), hip spica cast (older infants)

#### Scoliosis
- Lateral curvature of spine
- **Screening**: Adams forward bend test
- **Treatment**: Observation, bracing (Boston brace), surgery if severe (>40-50°)

### Treatments

#### Cast Care
- Elevate limb first 24-48hr
- Neurovascular checks: color, temp, cap refill, sensation, movement
- Keep dry; no foreign objects inside
- Report: increased pain, swelling, numbness, foul odor

#### Traction
- **Skin traction**: Buck's, Russell's — temporary
- **Skeletal traction**: Pins through bone — longer-term
- **Nursing**: Weights hanging freely, ropes aligned, countertraction maintained

#### Bracing
- Used for scoliosis, clubfoot, DDH
- Teach skin care, proper fit, wear schedule

### Common Nursing Interventions
| Situation | Intervention |
|-----------|--------------|
| Fracture with cast | Elevate, ice, neurovascular checks q1-2hr |
| Post-op DDH/SCFE | Position per orders; pain management; PT referral |
| Scoliosis brace | Ensure correct fit; skin checks; encourage compliance |
| Muscular dystrophy | Supportive care; respiratory monitoring; mobility aids |

## 🎯 KEY NURSING CONCEPTS (Cross-Chapter)

### Developmental Considerations
- Always consider child's developmental stage when planning care
- Age-appropriate communication and preparation
- Involve parents/family in care decisions

### Safety
- Car seats, bike helmets, water safety (adolescents: driving, drowning)
- Medication safety (childproof caps, storage)
- Fall prevention (cribs, high chairs, hospital beds)

### Family-Centered Care
- Parents are experts on their child
- Provide emotional support and education
- Encourage parental presence during procedures

### Pain Management
- Use age-appropriate tools
- Combine pharmacologic + non-pharmacologic approaches
- Reassess after interventions
