=== SLIDE 2 ===
Male Reproductive System – Internal Structures
LVN Term 3 – Reproductive System  |  Linton Med-Surg Nursing  (Ch. 51–54)
Testes
Two oval male gonads; develop in abdomen → descend into scrotum by birth; contain seminiferous tubules (sperm) and Leydig cells (testosterone)
Epididymis
Coiled tubule (~20 feet uncoiled) attached to posterior testes; sperm mature here over several days, gaining motility and fertilization capacity
Vas Deferens
Paired muscular tubes; primary sperm storage; propels sperm into ejaculatory duct during emission (peristalsis); vasectomy cuts/occludes here
Seminal Vesicles
Two glands posterior to bladder; produce alkaline, fructose-rich fluid (~60% of semen); provides nutrients for sperm motility and viability
Prostate Gland
Walnut-sized gland; surrounds urethra at bladder neck; secretes thin, milky, alkaline fluid (~30% of semen) — enhances sperm motility; enlarges with age (BPH)
Cowper (Bulbourethral) Glands
Two pea-sized glands below prostate; secrete clear mucus into urethra before ejaculation — lubrication and neutralization of urethral acidity
Ejaculatory Ducts
Formed by union of vas deferens + seminal vesicle duct; open into prostatic urethra; propel semen into urethra at ejaculation

Male Reproductive Physiology – Spermatogenesis & Sexual Response
LVN Term 3 – Reproductive System  |  Linton Med-Surg Nursing  (Ch. 51–54)
Spermatogenesis
Begins at puberty (~age 13) and continues throughout a man's lifetime
Process: testosterone triggers division of germinal epithelial cells in seminiferous tubules → spermatocytes → spermatids → mature sperm
Full cycle takes approximately 75 days
Temperature is critical: testicular temp must be ~3°C BELOW core body temp
↑ Testicular temperature → degeneration of seminiferous tubule cells → ↓ sperm production
Cryptorchidism (undescended testes): if uncorrected → permanent sterility
Erection & Ejaculation
Erection: parasympathetic stimulation → cavernosal arterioles relax → blood fills corpora cavernosa → penis expands 8–10× flaccid volume
Emission: sympathetic stimulation → smooth muscle contraction → sperm propelled to ejaculatory ducts; mixes with seminal/prostatic fluid
Ejaculation: rhythmic contractions of internal genital organs → expulsion of semen (~2–5 mL per ejaculate)
Internal urethral sphincter closes during ejaculation → urine and semen NEVER mix
Refractory period follows — varies by age and individual
Testosterone – Functions
Primary androgen produced by Leydig cells in the testes
Triggers development of primary sex characteristics (fetal) and secondary sex characteristics (puberty): voice deepening, body hair, muscle mass, genital growth, libido
Regulated by HPG axis: Hypothalamus → GnRH → Anterior Pituitary → LH/FSH → Testes → Testosterone → negative feedback to hypothalamus/pituitary
Testosterone levels peak in early adulthood and gradually decline after age 30–40

Physiology of the Male Reproductive System
Erection: The neurophysiological control of the erectile process is under the influence of central and peripheral processes. At least three kinds of erection can be distinguished in man: central, reflexogenic, and nocturnal types
Emission and ejaculation: Emission, is the first phase of ejaculation, a sympathetic spinal cord reflex
Spermatogenesis:

The spermatozoa undergo final maturation in the epididymis and are stored there prior to ejaculation

Acute or Chronic Bacterial Prostatitis
Acute bacterial prostatitis, a bacterial infection of the prostate usually with sudden, severe symptoms
Acute bacterial prostatitis is treated with antibiotics, analgesics, anti-inflammatory drugs, and Sitz baths for a period of 4 weeks
Chronic bacterial prostatitis, ongoing or recurring bacterial infection usually with less severe symptoms
Chronic prostatitis requires a 16-week period of antibiotic therapy

=== SLIDE 22 ===
Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia
Signs and Symptoms:
Classified as voiding, storage, or postmicturition 
Voiding symptoms include hesitancy, weak urinary stream, straining, and prolonged voiding
Frequency, urgency, nocturia, urge incontinence, and voiding small volumes are storage symptoms
Postmicturition symptoms are postvoid dribbling and incomplete emptying of the bladder

=== SLIDE 23 ===
Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia (BPH)
Diagnosis: 
Based on reported symptoms and findings from a rectal examination, endoscopy, ultrasound, catheterization for residual urine, and sometimes urodynamic testing 
Laboratory and radiographic studies include urinalysis, uroflowmetry, measurement of postvoid residual by ultrasound, and serum prostate specific antigen (PSA) 
If hematuria is present, cystoscopy may be done to rule out bladder cancer
Treatment:
Conservative measures can decrease urinary symptoms. These include maintaining fluid intake of 1500 to 2000 mL/day, fluid restriction for 2 hours before bedtime, avoidance of caffeine and alcohol, and some bladder training exercises
Drugs that may be prescribed to treat benign prostatic hyperplasia and related symptoms include 5-alpha reductase inhibitors, alpha1-adrenergic receptor antagonists (alpha1 blockers), and anticholinergics. 5-Alpha-reductase inhibitors suppress prostatic tissue growth by decreasing testosterone levels

=== SLIDE 24 ===
Prostatectomy
Prostatectomy
Is surgery to remove part or all of the prostate gland
The prostate gland is situated in the male pelvis, below the urinary bladder 
It surrounds the urethra, which carries urine from the bladder to the penis
The procedure is used to treat a number of conditions affecting the prostate
Transurethral resection of the prostate (TURP)
Most widely used surgical procedure Transurethral resection of the prostate is a urological operation
It is used to treat benign prostatic hyperplasia 
As the name indicates, it is performed by visualizing the prostate through the urethra and removing tissue by electrocautery or sharp dissection

=== SLIDE 25 ===
Nursing care (TURP)
An 18 gauge indwelling urinary or larger, will be inserted following surgery to irrigate blood clots and monitor urinary output
The client may have impotence following a prostatectomy. A more common complication is retrograde ejaculation of seminal fluid during intercourse, which causes the semen to go into the bladder instead of through the urethra
The nurse should instruct the client to drink at least 3 L of fluids daily to help decrease urinary tract infection and reduce dysuria
The client may resume a regular diet following surgery and the nurse should encourage a diet high in fiber to decrease constipation and straining during bowel movements. Constipation and straining can lead to increase bleeding from the prostate into the urine

=== SLIDE 26 ===
Prostatic Cancer
A cancer in a man's prostate, a small walnut-sized gland that produces seminal fluid.
A man's prostate produces the seminal fluid that nourishes and transports sperm.
Symptoms include difficulty with urination, but sometimes there are no symptoms at all.
Some types of prostate cancer grow slowly. In some of these cases, monitoring is recommended. Other types are aggressive and require radiation, surgery, hormone therapy, chemotherapy, or other treatments.

=== SLIDE 27 ===
Prostatic Cancer
Early detection
Cancer of the prostate is found on postmortem examination in 30% of men older than 50 years, and the incidence increases steadily with each decade to 100% of men in the tenth decade. 
PSA levels over 4 ng/L may indicate cancer of the prostate; further studies are needed
Medical treatment
The treatment of prostatic cancer is controversial because of the difficulty in staging tumors and the unpredictable biologic behavior of the disease

Testicular Self-Examination (TSE)
Testicular exam should begin at age 15 and continue through age 40. It is important to do the exam every month in order to find any changes. The best time to examine the testicles is right after a hot bath or shower
When the patient is warm and the scrotum is relaxed. The left testicle is usually lower than the right. The testicles should feel firm and smooth without lumps

=== SLIDE 32 ===
Peyronie Disease
A hard, nonelastic, fibrous tissue (plaque) just under the skin of the penis of men between 45 and 70 years of age 
Plaque develops as a result of an injury that causes inflammation and scarring of the tunica surrounding the corpora cavernosa

=== SLIDE 33 ===
Peyronie Disease
Loss of elasticity of the tunica results in decreased ability to fill during an erection and failure to store because of low pressure on the veins against the covering of the erectile tissue
The plaque is usually located on the dorsal midline surface of the penis and results in an upward bending of the penis during erection that may be painful and interfere with successful vaginal penetration
Treatment depends on the size of the plaque and the curvature and resultant degree of dysfunction. Drug Treatment, Radiation therapy and Surgical correction involves excision of the plaque.

=== SLIDE 34 ===
Priapism
Prolonged erection not related to sexual desire 
Causes
Injury to the penis, sickle cell crisis, and neoplasms of the brain or spinal cord 
Drugs that may be responsible include phenothiazines, alpha-adrenergic blockers, anticoagulants, alcohol, cocaine, marijuana, vardenafil (Levitra), and intracavernosal injections
Painful; constitutes an emergency situation 
Failure to resolve the problem within 12 to 24 hours may result in penile ischemia, gangrene, fibrosis, and erectile dysfunction

=== SLIDE 35 ===
Priapism: Treatment and Nursing care
Immediate removal of blood may be accomplished by aspirating blood from the erectile chambers or by injecting drugs that cause contraction of smooth muscle, inhibiting inflow of blood and allowing outflow. If these efforts fail, emergency surgery may be needed

Nursing care must be particularly sensitive to the embarrassment the patient may experience. Understanding the condition and alleviating pain are important

=== SLIDE 37 ===
Phimosis and Paraphimosis
Phimosis: result of poor hygiene, the foreskin may become inflamed and swollen, preventing retraction of the foreskin. Treated with antimicrobial agents and proper cleansing 
Paraphimosis: is the opposite in that the foreskin is retracted and cannot be moved over the glans. Constriction of the penis causes the glans to swell

=== SLIDE 38 ===
Infertility
Couples who have had unprotected intercourse over a 12-month period and have been unable to become pregnant 
May be caused by a reproductive problem in the male, the female, or both
Cause and risk factors
It is a common congenital condition, being found in approximately 30% of preterm male infants and in 1.0% to 3.4% of full-term male infants.
Cryptorchidism must be corrected within the ﬁrst 18 months of life to give the best chance for fertility. 
Men with undescended testes have a 10 to 30 times higher incidence of testicular cancer than men whose testes descended normally.
Vasectomy is usually performed as an outpatient procedure in a provider’s office or outpatient clinic.

=== SLIDE 39 ===
Infertility
Male infertility can be caused by low sperm production, abnormal sperm function or blockages that prevent the delivery of sperm. Illnesses, injuries, chronic health problems, lifestyle choices and other factors may contribute to male infertility
Semen analysis also called a sperm count, measures the quantity and quality of semen and sperm
The nurse should instruct the client to abstain from ejaculation for at least 2 to 5 days prior to the test

=== SLIDE 40 ===
Testicular Torsion
Testicular torsion occurs when a testicle rotates, twisting the spermatic cord that brings blood to the scrotum
The reduced blood flow causes sudden and often severe pain and swelling
Testicular torsion is most common between ages 12 and 18, but it can occur at any age, even before birth

=== SLIDE 41 ===
Varicocele
Lengthening and enlargement of the scrotal portion of the venous system that drains the testicle
Caused by incompetent or absent valves in the spermatic venous system, which allows pooled blood and the resulting increased pressure to dilate the veins. Most often, only the left testicle is affected
Affected testicles may be smaller and may have reduced spermatogenesis
Varicoceles are a factor in about half of male infertility cases 
Treatment includes surgical ligation or injection of a sclerosing agent into the dilated vein. Varicoceles may reappear after surgery and fertility may or may not improve

=== SLIDE 42 ===
Penile Cancer
Relatively rare; almost exclusively in uncircumcised men 
Risk factors: chronic irritation, poor hygiene, a history of multiple sexual partners, sexually transmitted infection, and long-term tobacco use 
A dry, wartlike painless growth on the penis that does not respond to antibiotic therapy
Can be removed surgically if treated in early stages 
Advanced stages may ulcerate and involve the foreskin and penile shaft 
Extensive resection or amputation as well as resection of nearby lymph nodes may be necessary

=== SLIDE 43 ===
Testicular Cancer
A painless palpable mass or enlargement of one testis is the most common symptom of testicular cancer
A feeling of heaviness and pain in the testicle occurs as the tumor grows

=== SLIDE 44 ===
Testicular Cancer
Testicular germ cell carcinoma occurs most often in young men between the ages of 15 and 35 years 
Among the risk factors for this type of cancer are cryptorchidism, a genetic abnormality, HIV infection, and acquired immunodeficiency syndrome (AIDS) (see the Cultural Considerations box)

=== SLIDE 46 ===
Male Reproductive System – External Structures
LVN Term 3 – Reproductive System  |  Linton Med-Surg Nursing  (Ch. 51–54)
Scrotum
Pendulous sac of skin posterior to penis
Contains the testes, epididymis, and lower spermatic cord
Regulates testicular temperature: maintains ~3°C BELOW core body temperature
Temperature regulation is ESSENTIAL for spermatogenesis
Cremasteric muscle contracts in cold → draws testes toward body; relaxes in heat
Penis
External organ composed of three cylinders of erectile tissue
Two corpora cavernosa: fill with blood during erection
One corpus spongiosum: surrounds urethra; forms glans (acorn-shaped tip)
Foreskin (prepuce): loose skin over glans; circumcision = surgical removal
Urethra: dual-purpose — carries urine and semen (never simultaneously)
Urethral meatus: external opening at tip of glans
Spermatic Cord & Supporting Structures
Spermatic cord: passes through inguinal canal; contains vas deferens, testicular arteries, veins (pampiniform plexus), lymphatics, and nerves
Pampiniform venous plexus: network of veins that helps regulate testicular temperature through countercurrent heat exchange
⚠️ Varicocele (enlarged pampiniform veins) can raise testicular temperature → impair sperm production → common cause of male infertility
Inguinal canal: pathway through abdominal wall — weakness here can lead to inguinal hernia

=== SLIDE 47 ===
Male Reproductive Assessment & Age-Related Changes
LVN Term 3 – Reproductive System  |  Linton Med-Surg Nursing  (Ch. 51–54)
Health History – Male
Chief complaint: pain, urinary symptoms, scrotal masses, penile discharge or lesions
Sexual history: STI history, multiple partners, ED, changes in libido
Fertility: concerns about conception, prior evaluation
Past medical history: DM, cardiovascular disease, neurologic disorders, prostate issues
Medications: antihypertensives (many cause ED), spironolactone, corticosteroids
Family history: prostate cancer, DM, hypertension, genetic disorders
Physical Examination
Penis: inspect for lesions, ulcers, discharge, urethral meatus position (hypospadias/epispadias)
Scrotum: palpate testes bilaterally for size, symmetry, consistency, tenderness, masses
Epididymis: palpate for tenderness or swelling (epididymitis)
Inguinal region: assess for hernias or enlarged lymph nodes
DRE (Digital Rectal Exam): provider palpates prostate through rectum — assesses size, shape, consistency, nodules
Age-Related Changes (Male Climacteric / Andropause)
Rapid decline in testosterone production after age 50
May experience hot flashes, feelings of suffocation, psychological changes (similar to female menopause)
Slower sexual arousal; longer refractory period between erections
Decrease in sperm motility; ↑ time to achieve orgasm
Prostate gland enlarges (BPH) — compresses urethra → urinary symptoms
⚠️ In a HEALTHY man: ability to have erections and father children can persist throughout life
Nursing Approach
Sexual history is sensitive — provide private, non-judgmental environment
Use open-ended, neutral language; avoid assumptions about sexuality or relationships
Normalize age-related changes while distinguishing from pathological conditions
Assess for depression or anxiety related to sexual dysfunction
Provide education on when to seek professional evaluation

=== SLIDE 48 ===
Diagnostic Tests – Male Reproductive System
LVN Term 3 – Reproductive System  |  Linton Med-Surg Nursing  (Ch. 51–54)
Semen Analysis
Purpose: Assess male fertility (post-vasectomy or infertility); evaluates: volume, count, motility, morphology (shape)
🩺 Nursing: Abstain from sexual activity 2–3 days before; collect by masturbation into sterile container; bring to lab within 1 hour at room temperature; keep warm (not hot)
PSA (Prostate-Specific Antigen)
Purpose: Screening for prostate cancer (controversial); normal: 0–4 ng/mL; elevated in cancer, BPH, and prostatitis; ACS: discuss at 50 (avg risk), 45 (high risk), 40 (highest risk)
🩺 Nursing: No special prep; explain limitations: high PSA ≠ cancer; normal PSA does not rule out cancer; shared decision-making approach
Transrectal Ultrasound (TRUS)
Purpose: Most accurate anatomic detail of prostate; guides biopsy to specific areas; also evaluates seminal vesicles
🩺 Nursing: Ultrasound probe inserted into rectum; no full bladder needed; patient may feel uncomfortable/fullness; explain procedure beforehand
Endocrinologic Studies
Purpose: LH, FSH, testosterone, prolactin levels; diagnose hypothalamic-pituitary dysfunction causing reproductive problems or ED
🩺 Nursing: Venipuncture; apply pressure and dressing; assess for bleeding; fasting may be required depending on panel ordered
Urethral Smears / Cultures
Purpose: Identify pathogens including STIs; susceptibility testing for antimicrobial selection
🩺 Nursing: Sterile swab inserted into urethra; patient lies down (may trigger vasovagal response); handle per Standard Precautions; label immediately
Cystoscopy
Purpose: Lighted flexible or rigid scope to visualize urethra, bladder, prostatic urethra; detects hyperplasia, bladder tumors, strictures
🩺 Nursing: Signed informed consent required; urine may be pink-tinged for 24 hrs post-procedure; monitor for inability to void; antibiotics as ordered

=== SLIDE 50 ===
Prostatitis & Epididymitis
LVN Term 3 – Reproductive System  |  Linton Med-Surg Nursing  (Ch. 51–54)
Prostatitis
Inflammation of prostate; can be acute bacterial, chronic bacterial, or chronic pelvic pain syndrome
Cause: E. coli, Klebsiella, Proteus (Gram-negative bacteria); STIs (Chlamydia, gonorrhea) in younger men
S&S: pelvic/perineal/ejaculatory pain; voiding problems (urgency, frequency, hesitancy); fever and chills in acute bacterial
Treatment: antibiotics (fluoroquinolones, TMP-SMX for 4–6 weeks); alpha blockers for voiding; NSAIDs; sitz baths; stool softeners
Nursing: encourage fluids; sitz baths 3×/day; avoid spicy food, caffeine, alcohol; complete full antibiotic course
Epididymitis
Infection/inflammation of epididymis; most common cause of scrotal pain in adult men
Cause: Chlamydia trachomatis or N. gonorrhoeae in men <35; E. coli in older men
S&S: Gradual onset of painful scrotal edema; nausea, vomiting, chills, fever; scrotal skin may be red/warm
Treatment: bed rest; scrotal elevation and support (scrotal support/jockstrap); ice packs 20 min on/off; analgesics; antibiotics (treat STI cause); partner must be treated
Nursing: assess pain; educate on STI prevention; enforce scrotal elevation; no heavy lifting
⚠️  Untreated epididymitis can progress to orchitis (infection of testes) → testicular atrophy → infertility. Emphasize completing antibiotic treatment.
💡  Distinguish epididymitis from testicular torsion: epididymitis = gradual onset, relief with scrotal elevation (Prehn's sign+); torsion = sudden onset, NO relief with elevation = EMERGENCY

=== SLIDE 51 ===
Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia (BPH)
LVN Term 3 – Reproductive System  |  Linton Med-Surg Nursing  (Ch. 51–54)
Pathophysiology
Non-malignant enlargement of the prostate gland — extremely common with aging; affects >50% of men over age 60
Prostate surrounds the urethra at the bladder neck → enlargement compresses the urethra → obstructs urine outflow
Exact cause unknown; testosterone (dihydrotestosterone/DHT) is a key driver; estrogen may also play a role
BPH does NOT become prostate cancer but both can coexist; PSA may be elevated with BPH
Clinical Manifestations (Obstructive + Irritative)
Urinary hesitancy (difficulty starting stream)
Weak or intermittent urinary stream
Straining to void
Sensation of incomplete bladder emptying
Nocturia (waking 2+ times to urinate at night)
Post-void dribbling
Recurrent UTIs from urinary retention
In severe cases: acute urinary retention (unable to void at all) = EMERGENCY
Treatment
Watchful waiting: mild symptoms; annual monitoring
5-alpha reductase inhibitors: finasteride (Proscar), dutasteride (Avodart) → shrink prostate (takes months)
Alpha-1 adrenergic blockers: tamsulosin (Flomax), terazosin → relax smooth muscle → ↑ urine flow
Minimally invasive: TUMT (microwave), TUNA (radiofrequency ablation)
Surgery: TURP (transurethral resection of prostate) = gold standard for moderate-severe BPH
Post-TURP: urine pink-tinged → monitor for clot retention; bladder irrigation as ordered

=== SLIDE 52 ===
Structural Male Disorders – Phimosis, Paraphimosis & Testicular Torsion
LVN Term 3 – Reproductive System  |  Linton Med-Surg Nursing  (Ch. 51–54)
Phimosis
Cause: Congenital or acquired from repeated infections/inflammation; foreskin cannot be retracted over glans
S&S: Difficulty with hygiene under foreskin; may obstruct urine stream; ↑ risk of infections and penile cancer
Tx: Manual dilation; topical corticosteroid cream; dorsal slit (emergency); circumcision (definitive treatment)
Nursing: Teach importance of foreskin hygiene in uncircumcised males; gentle daily retraction and cleaning if manageable
Paraphimosis
Cause: Foreskin retracted and left behind glans — becomes trapped; constricts glans; ischemia can develop rapidly
S&S: Painful edema of glans; tight ring of foreskin behind glans; erythema; if untreated → tissue necrosis
Tx: ⚠️ UROLOGIC EMERGENCY: manual reduction with lubrication; ice to reduce edema; surgical dorsal slit or emergency circumcision if manual reduction fails
Nursing: Always restore foreskin to natural position after catheterization or exam; never leave retracted
Testicular Torsion
Cause: Spermatic cord twists → cuts off blood supply to testis; 'bell-clapper' deformity allows twisting; most common ages 12–18
S&S: SUDDEN, severe unilateral scrotal pain; swollen, elevated, tender testicle; nausea, vomiting; no fever initially
Tx: ⚠️ SURGICAL EMERGENCY: detorsion within 4–6 hours = 90% testis salvage; delayed >24 hrs = near-certain loss; orchiopexy (fixation) of BOTH testes
Nursing: Do NOT delay — immediate provider notification; prepare for emergency surgery; manage pain; NPO if surgery anticipated

=== SLIDE 53 ===
Erectile Dysfunction (ED)
LVN Term 3 – Reproductive System  |  Linton Med-Surg Nursing  (Ch. 51–54)
Definition & Causes
ED: persistent inability to achieve or maintain an erection sufficient for satisfactory sexual intercourse
Psychological causes: anxiety, depression, relationship issues, performance anxiety
Physiological causes: vascular disease (most common), DM (neuropathy/vascular), neurologic disorders, hormonal deficiencies, pelvic surgery
Medications causing ED: antihypertensives (beta-blockers, thiazides), antidepressants, antipsychotics, antiandrogens, alcohol/substance use
Treatment Options
PDE5 inhibitors: sildenafil (Viagra), tadalafil (Cialis), vardenafil (Levitra), avanafil (Stendra) — first-line oral therapy
Intracavernosal injections: alprostadil directly into corpus cavernosum
Intraurethral suppository (MUSE): alprostadil pellet inserted into urethra
Vacuum erection device (VED): mechanical suction device
Penile implant (prosthesis): inflatable or semi-rigid; surgical; last resort
Testosterone replacement: only if documented deficiency
Nursing Care
Approach with sensitivity — affects self-image and relationships
Review all medications for potential ED side effects
⚠️ PDE5 inhibitors: CONTRAINDICATED with nitrates (nitroglycerin, isosorbide) → fatal hypotension
PDE5 inhibitors only work WITH sexual stimulation; not an aphrodisiac
Educate patient and partner together when possible
Assess for depression or relationship distress; refer for counseling
Lifestyle modifications: smoking cessation, weight loss, exercise, ↓ alcohol
⚠️  NEVER administer PDE5 inhibitors (Viagra, Cialis, Levitra) to a patient who is taking nitrate medications (nitroglycerin, isosorbide). This combination causes severe, potentially fatal hypotension.

=== SLIDE 54 ===
Prostate Cancer – Risk Factors & Clinical Manifestations
LVN Term 3 – Reproductive System  |  Linton Med-Surg Nursing  (Ch. 51–54)
Risk Factors
Age: most cases diagnosed after age 65; uncommon before 40
Race: African American men have highest incidence AND mortality rates in the US
Family history: first-degree relative with prostate cancer doubles risk
High-fat diet (especially saturated fat from red meat)
BRCA2 gene mutation increases risk
Hormone-related: excess testosterone or androgen exposure
Screening Recommendations (ACS)
Age 50: men at AVERAGE risk with 10+ year life expectancy
Age 45: men at HIGH risk (African American; father/brother with prostate Ca before 65)
Age 40: men at HIGHEST risk (more than one first-degree relative with prostate Ca)
Screening tools: PSA blood test + Digital Rectal Exam (DRE)
PSA screening is CONTROVERSIAL — discuss risks/benefits with each patient
⚠️ Elevated PSA does NOT confirm cancer; also elevated in BPH and prostatitis
Clinical Manifestations
EARLY STAGE: usually completely ASYMPTOMATIC
Grows slowly — often discovered incidentally via PSA or DRE
Urinary symptoms (similar to BPH): hesitancy, weak stream, nocturia
Hematuria (blood in urine) or hematospermia (blood in semen)
ADVANCED disease: bone pain (metastasis to spine/pelvis), weight loss, anemia, fatigue
Lymphedema of lower extremities if pelvic lymph node involvement
Diagnosis & Staging
PSA + DRE (firm, irregular, hard nodule on prostate = suspicious)
Transrectal ultrasound (TRUS) guided biopsy: definitive diagnosis
Gleason score (1–10): grades tumor aggressiveness (higher = more aggressive)
Staging: T1 (not palpable) → T4 (invades adjacent structures)
Bone scan, CT, MRI: evaluate for metastatic spread
TNM staging system: Tumor size, Node involvement, Metastasis

=== SLIDE 55 ===
Prostate Cancer – Treatment & Nursing Care
LVN Term 3 – Reproductive System  |  Linton Med-Surg Nursing  (Ch. 51–54)
Treatment Options
Active surveillance: low-grade, localized cancer; PSA every 3–6 months; avoid treatment side effects
Radical prostatectomy: surgical removal of prostate; laparoscopic or robotic-assisted (da Vinci)
Radiation: external beam radiation therapy (EBRT) or brachytherapy (radioactive seed implants)
Androgen deprivation therapy (ADT): GnRH agonists (Lupron, Zoladex) → ↓ testosterone
Androgen receptor blockers: bicalutamide (Casodex), flutamide
CYP17 inhibitor: abiraterone (Zytiga) + prednisone — blocks androgen synthesis
Immunotherapy: sipuleucel-T (Provenge) for metastatic prostate cancer
Post-Prostatectomy Nursing Care
Monitor urinary output: indwelling catheter typically stays in place for 7–14 days
Urinary incontinence is common post-surgery — usually resolves over weeks to months
Teach Kegel exercises (pelvic floor strengthening) — begin when catheter removed
Bladder retraining: scheduled voiding; decrease urge incontinence
Assess for urinary retention after catheter removal
Wound care: assess incision for signs of infection
Pain management: analgesics as ordered; assess effectiveness
Side Effects of Treatment
GnRH agonists: hot flashes, ↓ libido, ↓ muscle mass, ↑ adipose tissue, gynecomastia, osteoporosis
Androgen blockers: hepatotoxicity, nausea, gynecomastia, hot flashes, ↓ libido
Radiation: fatigue, bowel/bladder irritation (proctitis, cystitis), sexual dysfunction
Surgery: erectile dysfunction (nerve-sparing technique helps), incontinence
⚠️ Monitor liver function with antiandrogens: jaundice, dark urine, right-side pain
Psychosocial Nursing Care
Erectile dysfunction is a major concern post-treatment — address directly and compassionately
Loss of sexual function affects quality of life, self-image, and relationships
Involve partner in discussions when patient desires
Refer to support groups: Us TOO International (prostate cancer survivor network)
Discuss treatment options for ED: PDE5 inhibitors, penile prosthesis, vacuum device
⚠️ PDE5 inhibitors remain CONTRAINDICATED with nitrates — even in cancer context

=== SLIDE 56 ===
Testicular Cancer
LVN Term 3 – Reproductive System  |  Linton Med-Surg Nursing  (Ch. 51–54)
Pathophysiology & Risk Factors
Most common solid tumor in males aged 15–35 years; highly curable if detected early
Most are germ cell tumors: seminomas (slow-growing, radiosensitive) or non-seminomas (teratoma, embryonal carcinoma, yolk sac)
Risk factors: cryptorchidism (undescended testicle) is #1 risk factor; family history; Klinefelter syndrome; prior testicular cancer in other testis
Incidence higher in Caucasian men; rare in African American men
Clinical Manifestations & Diagnosis
Usually PAINLESS, firm, fixed lump or nodule on testis
Sensation of heaviness or aching in lower abdomen or scrotum
Sudden accumulation of fluid in scrotum (hydrocele)
Gynecomastia: ↑ HCG from tumor stimulates breast tissue
Diagnosis: testicular ultrasound (first step); tumor markers: AFP, beta-HCG, LDH
CT abdomen/pelvis/chest: staging and lymph node involvement
Orchiectomy: surgical removal of affected testis — both diagnostic AND therapeutic
Treatment & Nursing Care
Radical orchiectomy (inguinal approach): definitive treatment
Seminoma: orchiectomy + radiation ± chemotherapy (BEP: bleomycin, etoposide, cisplatin)
Non-seminoma: orchiectomy + retroperitoneal lymph node dissection ± chemotherapy
Cure rate >90% for early-stage disease — encourage early presentation
Nursing: discuss sperm banking BEFORE treatment (radiation/chemo can cause sterility)
Teach testicular self-examination (TSE): monthly after age 15; best performed after warm shower
Address body image, fertility concerns, sexual function honestly

=== SLIDE 57 ===
Pharmacology – Male Reproductive System (Part 1)
LVN Term 3 – Reproductive System  |  Linton Med-Surg Nursing  (Ch. 51–54)
Androgens (Testosterone)
Examples: Androderm patch, AndroGel, Testopel pellet, testosterone IM (cypionate/enanthate)
Use: Hormone deficiency, hypogonadism, impotence, anemia, delayed puberty; females: menopausal symptoms, advanced breast cancer
Side Effects: Thrombosis, stroke, MI, sodium/water retention, virilization in females, polycythemia, testicular atrophy (suppresses natural production), acne
Nursing: Z-track IM technique; Androderm patch: arm/back/abdomen — NEVER on scrotum; gels: let dry before dressing — children/pregnant women must AVOID contact; watch for mood changes/aggression
5-Alpha Reductase Inhibitors (BPH)
Examples: Finasteride (Proscar/Propecia), Dutasteride (Avodart)
Use: Reduce prostate size by blocking conversion of testosterone to DHT; BPH treatment; also treats male pattern baldness (lower dose Propecia)
Side Effects: ↓ Ejaculate volume, ↓ libido, ↓ PSA (lowers PSA up to 50% — must adjust interpretation), ED, teratogenic for male fetus
Nursing: ⚠️ Pregnant women must NOT handle tablets (absorbed through skin → fetal harm); effects are not immediate — may take 3–6 months; monitor PSA (multiply result by 2 as adjustment)
Alpha-1 Adrenergic Antagonists (BPH)
Examples: Tamsulosin (Flomax), Terazosin (Hytrin), Doxazosin (Cardura), Alfuzosin (Uroxatral)
Use: Relax smooth muscle of bladder neck, prostate capsule, and urethra → ↓ urinary obstruction → improved urine flow; symptom relief within days
Side Effects: Orthostatic hypotension (most important), dizziness, rhinitis, abnormal ejaculation (retrograde); ⚠️ Intraoperative floppy iris syndrome (IFIS) during cataract surgery
Nursing: Take at bedtime to minimize dizziness; rise slowly; monitor BP; MUST tell ophthalmologist before any eye surgery; lifelong therapy likely
PDE5 Inhibitors (Erectile Dysfunction)
Examples: Sildenafil (Viagra), Tadalafil (Cialis), Vardenafil (Levitra), Avanafil (Stendra)
Use: Inhibit phosphodiesterase-5 → relax corpus cavernosum smooth muscle → ↑ blood flow → erection; ONLY works with sexual stimulation
Side Effects: Headache, flushing, dizziness, abnormal vision (blue tinge), nasal congestion, diarrhea; ⚠️ SUDDEN DEATH if taken with organic nitrates
Nursing: ⚠️ ABSOLUTE CONTRAINDICATION with nitrates; do not take >1× per day; onset 30–60 min (Stendra: 15 min); advise against grapefruit juice (↑ drug levels)

=== SLIDE 58 ===
Pharmacology – Male Reproductive System (Part 2)
LVN Term 3 – Reproductive System  |  Linton Med-Surg Nursing  (Ch. 51–54)
GnRH Agonists / LHRH Analogs
Examples: Leuprolide (Lupron), Goserelin (Zoladex), Triptorelin (Trelstar), Histrelin (Vantas)
Use: Desensitize pituitary GnRH receptors → ↓ LH/FSH → ↓ testosterone (medical castration); advanced prostate cancer, endometriosis, uterine fibroids
Side Effects: Hot flashes, impotence, ↓ libido, ↓ muscle mass, ↑ adipose tissue, bone pain on initiation (testosterone flare), osteoporosis with long-term use
Nursing: Leuprolide: SC or IM every 1/3/6/12 months; Goserelin (Zoladex): pellet injected into upper abdominal wall with 16-gauge needle — apply local anesthetic first; rotate injection sites
Androgen Receptor Blockers
Examples: Bicalutamide (Casodex), Flutamide (Eulexin), Nilutamide (Nilandron), Enzalutamide (Xtandi)
Use: Block androgen receptors → prevent testosterone/DHT from binding; used with LHRH analogs for combined androgen blockade in advanced prostate cancer
Side Effects: Hepatotoxicity ⚠️, gynecomastia, hot flashes, nausea, impotence, diarrhea, edema, confusion, depression; nilutamide: visual adaptation delay (dark→light)
Nursing: Monitor liver function tests (LFTs); teach signs of hepatotoxicity: jaundice, dark urine, right upper quadrant pain; do not double up missed doses; take consistently
CYP17 Inhibitors
Examples: Abiraterone (Zytiga) — always given WITH prednisone
Use: Inhibits CYP17 enzyme → blocks androgen synthesis in testes, adrenal glands, AND tumor tissue; more complete androgen suppression than LHRH alone; castration-resistant prostate cancer
Side Effects: Hypertension, hypokalemia, fluid retention, liver toxicity, adrenal insufficiency (requires prednisone co-administration), cardiac events
Nursing: Must take prednisone daily to prevent adrenal crisis (abiraterone blocks cortisol synthesis); monitor K+ and BP; LFTs monthly for first 3 months; take on empty stomach
Immunotherapy – Sipuleucel-T (Provenge)
Examples: Provenge (sipuleucel-T)
Use: Autologous cellular immunotherapy for asymptomatic/minimally symptomatic metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer; activates patient's own immune cells to target prostate cancer
Side Effects: Infusion reactions (chills, fever, fatigue within 1–2 days of infusion), cerebrovascular events (rare), back/joint pain
Nursing: Three infusions 2 weeks apart; patient's blood drawn, processed, and reinfused; not curative — extends median survival ~4 months; monitor for infusion reactions during and after each dose

=== SLIDE 59 ===
Infertility – Definition & Causes
LVN Term 3 – Reproductive System  |  Linton Med-Surg Nursing  (Ch. 51–54)
Defined: inability to conceive after 12 months of regular unprotected intercourse  |  Affects ~10% of American females ages 15–44; ~15% of couples overall
Female Causes of Infertility
Structural: uterine abnormalities, uterine fibroids, polyps, adhesions (Asherman syndrome)
Tubal factor: PID → fallopian tube scarring/obstruction (gonococcus, Chlamydia)
Ovulatory dysfunction: polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS), hypothalamic dysfunction, thyroid/adrenal disorders
Endometriosis: adhesions distort anatomy; endometrial implants on tubes/ovaries
Cervical factor: abnormal cervical mucus (hostile to sperm), cervical stenosis
Age-related: ↓ ovarian reserve, ↓ egg quality (especially after 35)
Other: malnutrition, severe anemia, chronic illness, extreme weight changes, anxiety
Male Causes of Infertility
Spermatogenesis failure: cryptorchidism, varicocele, orchitis (testicular damage from mumps)
Congenital: Klinefelter syndrome (47XXY), absent vas deferens, Y-chromosome microdeletion
Endocrine: hypogonadism, hypothyroidism, hyperprolactinemia
Obstructive: prior vasectomy, epididymitis scarring, ejaculatory duct obstruction
Behavioral/lifestyle: smoking, excessive alcohol, marijuana, anabolic steroids, hot baths, tight underwear
Medications: antihypertensives, spironolactone, corticosteroids, chemotherapy
Sexual dysfunction: ED, retrograde ejaculation
💡  Infertility evaluation should assess BOTH partners simultaneously — ~30% male factor only, ~30% female factor only, ~30% both, ~10% unexplained

=== SLIDE 60 ===
Infertility – Diagnosis, Treatment & Nursing Care
LVN Term 3 – Reproductive System  |  Linton Med-Surg Nursing  (Ch. 51–54)
Diagnostic Workup
Semen analysis: volume, count, motility, morphology; must abstain 2–3 days before
Basal body temperature (BBT) chart: identifies ovulation (temperature ↑ ~0.5°F at ovulation)
Ovulation prediction kits (OPK): detect LH surge in urine 24–36 hrs before ovulation
Hormone panel: FSH, LH, estradiol, progesterone, prolactin, thyroid, testosterone
Hysterosalpingography (HSG): X-ray with dye injected into uterus to visualize fallopian tube patency
Pelvic ultrasound: follicle tracking; uterine/ovarian anatomy
Laparoscopy: definitive visualization of pelvic organs; diagnose endometriosis, adhesions
Treatment Options
Ovulation induction: Clomid (clomiphene), menotropins (Repronex), gonadotropins
IUI (intrauterine insemination): washed sperm placed directly into uterus during ovulation
IVF (in vitro fertilization): eggs retrieved → fertilized in lab → embryo transferred to uterus
GIFT (gamete intrafallopian transfer): eggs + sperm placed directly into fallopian tube
ZIFT (zygote intrafallopian transfer): fertilized embryo placed into fallopian tube
Donor egg/sperm/embryo programs
Surgery: myomectomy, tubal reanastomosis, varicocele repair
Nursing Care
Privacy and sensitivity: reproductive health is deeply personal — always non-judgmental
Emotional support: fertility treatment is physically and emotionally exhausting
Low self-esteem: explore feelings; validate the emotional highs and lows
Altered sexual function: scheduled intercourse feels clinical — acknowledge this
Support groups and counseling referrals
Fertility Timing Teaching
Intercourse every other day around ovulation for best sperm availability
Viable sperm: 20–150 million per ejaculate (WHO normal ≥15 million/mL)
Sperm survival: 3–5 days in female reproductive tract
Ovum survival: ~12–24 hours after release — timing is critical
Avoid lubricants (most are spermicidal); warm baths immediately before intercourse
