=== SLIDE 11 ===
Bleeding disorders in pregnancy
Early pregnancy is a risky time for women and their embryos.
Bleeding may occur spontaneously and could represent the start of many possible undesired outcomes-such as a threatened abortion, ectopic pregnancy, hydatidiform mole. 
Bleeding is dangerous when ETIOLOGY remains unknown
Women and families get very scared because bleeding in pregnancy, while being a possible physiologic variant, is usually an ominous sign, or interpreted as one.

Procedures used in early pregnancy termination
Therapeutic abortion: intentional termination to preserve the health of the mother
Elective abortion: intentional termination of pregnancy for reasons other than the health of the mother
Vacuum aspiration (or curettage): cervical dilation, followed by controlled suction to remove all POC (Products of Conception) (up to 12 weeks)
D&E: Dilation of the cervix followed by gentle scraping of the uterine walls to remove POC (1st-2nd trimester) 
Mifepristone: oral medication can be taken, then a follow up prostaglandin agent is used which causes muscle contractions and termination of pregnancy in one’s own home. Follow up is vital with health care provider.

=== SLIDE 28 ===
Placenta previa vs. abruption?
There are two very distinct, important types of placental situations in pregnancy that can lead to emergencies and loss of life or massive amounts of blood.
These are Placenta previa and Placenta abruption
Placenta previa occurs when the placenta develops in the lower part of the uterus rather than the upper part. There are degrees of it. (marginal (reaches 2-3cm of cervix), partial (partly covers cervix), total (completely covers cervix)
Manifestations: painless bright red, vaginal bleeding.

=== SLIDE 36 ===
Disseminated intravascular coagulation aka DIC
What is DIC? Clot formation and destruction of clots co-occurs, becoming quickly life threatening as the mother’s body is deficient in clotting factors.
Clotting factors & blood are infused 
This is a life-threatening emergency.
With DIC: there is risk of maternal shock, death, clotting disorders, and fetal death.  
Warning signs could include signs of shock, bleeding from nose, gums, mouth, incisions, venipuncture sites
Rising size of uterus suggests blood accumulation, it is also typically tender & hard.

=== SLIDE 43 ===
Proteinuria?
As reduced blood flow occurs due to vasospasm impeding blood flow to the mother’s organs and placenta, some of these signs appear:
1. hypertension
2. edema
3. proteinuria
As kidneys get damaged by reduced blood flow, this damage allows protein to leak into the urine. 
Labs checked: hemoglobin, hematocrit, platelets, urine, serum creatinine, serum uric acid, serum transaminase (Table 5.6)

=== SLIDE 44 ===
Medications and treatment
Labetalol: antihypertensive of choice (contraindicated however if asthma/heart failure concurrent diagnoses)
Management of chronic hypertension that develops into preeclampsia : frequent fetal evaluations, ultrasounds, non stress tests
Possibly early delivery at 36-37 wks , timing is based on maternal/fetal status, prognosis
Patient instructed to report symptoms of worsening condition: headaches, visual changes, monitor abnormal lab results.

=== SLIDE 45 ===
Preeclampsia treatment
Treatment focuses on maintaining blood flow to the woman’s vital organs and placenta and
Preventing convulsions.
Birth is the cure for preeclampsia
Conservative treatment is sometimes allowed at home/hospital includes typically activity restriction, maternal assessment of fetal activity, blood pressure monitoring 2-4x/day, daily weights, checking urine for protein, monitoring FHTs may occur while in hospital continuously, intermittently or at home even.

=== SLIDE 47 ===
HELLP continued
HELLP syndrome is more common in preeclamptic women.
RUQ pain, epigastric pain, nausea, vomiting, malaise may all signal that HELLP syndrome is developing. 
Usually if patient gets HELLP they will be in the ICU and given magnesium sulfate to prevent convulsions and antihypertensives. Expedient delivery is performed at optimal time for fetal and maternal well being.
Bleeding is closely monitored. Most patients improve after delivery.

Pregnancy Complicated by Medical Conditions
Diabetes mellitus (DM) 
Classified if preceded pregnancy
Type 1: pathologic disorder
Type 2: insulin resistance; genetic predisposition
Pregestational DM: type 1 or 2 DM
Gestational DM (GDM)
Glucose intolerance with onset during pregnancy
In true GDM, glucose usually returns to normal by 6 weeks postpartum.

=== SLIDE 54 ===
Effect of Pregnancy on Glucose Metabolism
Hormones (estrogen and progesterone), insulinase (an enzyme), and increased prolactin levels have two effects
Increased resistance of cells to insulin
Increased speed of insulin breakdown

=== SLIDE 55 ===
Preexisting Diabetes Mellitus
Major risk for congenital anomalies to occur from maternal hyperglycemia during the embryonic period of development, for example cardiac defects like Tetralogy of Fallot.

=== SLIDE 56 ===
GDM RISKS in pregnancy
Maternal: Increased risks for any of the following: spontaneous abortion, gestational hypertension, preterm labor and PROM, polyhydramnios, infections, complications of large fetal size (aka macrosomia), ketoacidosis
Fetal: Increased risks for congenital abnormalities, complications of macrosomia, IUGR, birth injury, delayed lung maturation, respiratory distress syndrome, neonatal hypoglycemia, neonatal hypocalcemia, neonatal hyperbilirubinemia and jaundice, neonatal polycythemia, perinatal death.

Genetic Anemias (1 of 2)
Sickle cell disease
Autosomal recessive disorder
Abnormal hemoglobin 
Causes erythrocytes to become distorted sickle (crescent) shaped during hypoxic or acidotic episodes
Abnormally shaped blood cells do not flow smoothly
Can clog small blood vessels

=== SLIDE 76 ===

Can lead to preterm birth, growth restriction, and fetal demise

=== SLIDE 115 ===
In Summary
Pregnancy can be a very exciting and joyous event for many couples/families
However, it can also go very wrong and can dramatically cause changes in a woman’s health and well being as well as effect her infant’s survival
Awareness of causes of harm and avoidance are a start
Routine prenatal care and testing can identify many possible causes of harm early, or treat as soon as possible to improve best possible perinatal outcomes
