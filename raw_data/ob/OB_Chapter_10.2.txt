=== SLIDE 10 ===
Postpartum hemorrhage
Is traditionally defined as blood loss greater than 500mL after a vaginal birth (1000mL for a C/Section)
The major risk of hemorrhage is immediately hypovolemic shock, which interrupts blood flow to the body cells, preventing oxygenation, and nutrient delivery.  It is a leading cause of maternal mortality around the world.

=== SLIDE 11 ===
Maternal morbidity and mortality
Leading causes of death differ throughout pregnancy and after.
Heart disease and stroke cause most deaths overall.
Obstetric emergencies, like severe bleeding and amniotic fluid embolism (when amniotic fluid enters a mother’s bloodstream), cause most deaths at delivery.
In the week after delivery, severe bleeding, high blood pressure and infection are most common.
Cardiomyopathy (weakened heart muscle) is the leading cause of deaths 1 week to 1 year after delivery. (Cdc.gov)

Body’s Response to Hypovolemia
Initially: increased heart & respiratory rates
Purpose of response:
Increase oxygen content of RBC’s 
Speeds up circulation of remaining blood
Blood flow to nonessential organs decreases and stops eventually.
Skin & mucous membranes: pale, cold, & clammy.

=== SLIDE 24 ===
Uterine Atony vs. Subinvolution
Uterine atony describes a lack of normal muscle tone.  The uterus normally contracts with muscle fibers compressing bleeding vessels from placental site.
If the uterus is atonic, these muscle fibers are flaccid and don’t compress the vessels.
On palpation: the uterus is difficult to feel, and feels boggy or soft, with a high fundal height, often above the umbilicus.

=== SLIDE 25 ===
Subinvolution continued
Involution is the return of the uterus to its nonpregnant condition postpartum. 
The placental site heals by 6 wks postpartum.
Subinvolution is a slower than expected return of the uterus to its nonpregnant condition.
s/s: fundal height > expected, persistence of lochia rubra, pelvic pain, heaviness, fatigue.
Treatment: Methergine, antibiotics, D&C if necessary.

Fibrinogen levels increase during pregnancy, but clot-dissolving factors in the blood are normally decreased during pregnancy.

Thromboembolic Disorders (2 of 2)
Types of thromboembolic disorders
Superficial vein thrombosis (SVT)
(saphenous vein of lower leg)
Deep vein thrombosis (DVT)
(veins from feet to the femoral area, confirm diagnosis by ultrasound, especially if leg circumference > 2cm with redness, tenderness & edema)
Pulmonary embolism (PE)

=== SLIDE 42 ===
Occurs when the pulmonary artery is obstructed by a blood clot which embolizes (breaks off) and lodges in the lungs
S/S: Sudden chest pain, cough, dyspnea (SOB), decreased LOC, s/s heart failure
Small PE may have nonspecific S/S: SOB, palpitations, hemoptysis, faintness, low-grade fever

=== SLIDE 45 ===
Nursing Care to Prevent a Thromboembolism
Watch for signs or symptoms of PE
Dyspnea
Coughing
Chest pain
Avoid pressure in the popliteal space behind the knee.
Early ambulation and range-of-motion exercises.
If anti-embolic stockings are prescribed, such as with varicose veins, the nurse should teach the woman the correct method of putting on the stockings

=== SLIDE 46 ===
Treatment for thromboembolic DOs
For SVTs: analgesics, local application of heat, elevation of legs (promotes venous drainage)
DVTs: similar to SVTs, with the addition of anticoagulation drugs such as heparin or LMWH (Lovenox). 
PE: usually transferred to an ICU
Antibiotics are prescribed if infection is a factor.

=== SLIDE 50 ===
Infection—Puerperal Sepsis
An infection or septicemia after childbirth, with a fever of 38°C (100.4°F) within the first 24 hours and for at least 2 days during the first 10 days postpartum
Risks
Cracks in the nipples of the breasts 
Surgical incision
Tissue trauma during labor
Open wound at the placental insertion site
Retained placenta or blood clots 
Increased pH of the vagina after birth
Endometritis (inflammation of the lining of the uterus)

=== SLIDE 51 ===
The Dangers of Puerperal Infection
A localized infection of the perineum, vagina, or cervix can ascend into the reproductive tract and spread to the uterus, fallopian tubes, and peritoneum, causing peritonitis, a life-threatening condition.
A fever is most often caused by Endometritis (an inflammation of the inner lining of uterus) the blockage occurring due to retained placenta or clots increases infection susceptibility

=== SLIDE 52 ===
Nursing Care
The objective is to prevent the infection from occurring.
Use and teach hygienic measures.
Promote adequate rest and nutrition for healing.
Teach and observe for signs of infection.
Teach the woman how to correctly apply perineal pads (front to back).
Teach the woman to take all antimicrobial medications as prescribed.

=== SLIDE 58 ===
Treatment of Mastitis
Antibiotics & milk removal are cornerstones of treatment, analgesics prn.
Continue to breastfeed or pump if stops nursing. 
Weaning during mastitis will worsen symptoms (engorgement/milk stasis)
Heat promotes blood flow to the area, use warm shower or moist heat applications with warm packs pre-nursing.
Massage the area of inflammation to improve milk flow and reduce stasis.
Encourage fluid intake.
Wear a supportive bra.
Provide emotional support to the woman.
Apply cold compresses (ice pack) after feeding.  See Patient Teaching Box Pg. 256

Mood Disorders
Mood is defined as a pervasive and sustained emotion that can color one’s view of life.
Psychosis: serious impairment of one’s perception of reality. 
Postpartum depression: is a nonpsychotic depressive illness usually manifested within 2 to 4 weeks after delivery.

Nursing Tip

Explore her feelings, help to determine if they are persistent and/or  pervasive.
