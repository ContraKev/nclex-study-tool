A zygote is the single cell formed when a sperm fertilizes an egg, while a morula is a solid ball of 16–32 cells that develops from the zygote through rapid cell division called cleavage. The zygote is the very first stage after fertilization, whereas the morula is a later stage that precedes the blastula

=== SLIDE 28 ===
Zygote journeys on
The zygote (cell formed by union of sperm and ovum) travels into the uterus. 
As it travels it grows, and is dividing, by cleavage. Eventually it becomes the morula-the name of the cell (solid ball of cells) that enters the uterus by the 3rd day, and floats there another 2 to 4 days.
The cells form a cavity, and two layers evolve, an inner and outer layer.

Chorion and Amnion
Chorion develops from the trophoblast, and envelops the amnion, embryo and yolk sac
It eventually forms the fetal portion of the placenta
The amnion is the second membrane, the thin structure that envelops the embryo
Together: the chorion and amnion form an amniotic sac that encases the embryo. The volume of amniotic fluid steadily increases from about 30 mL at 10 weeks gestation to 350 mL at 20 wks, and 1000mL at 37 weeks.

=== SLIDE 55 ===
Fetal Circulation
After the fourth week of gestation, circulation of blood through the placenta to the fetus is occurring. There are three circulatory shunts to know about as they help override needed processes which aren’t developmentally ready to occur yet. 
1. Ductus venosus: diverts some blood away from the liver as it returns from the placenta
2. Foramen ovale: diverts most blood from the right atrium directly to the left atrium, rather than circulating it to the lungs
3. Ductus arteriosus:  Diverts most blood from the pulmonary artery into the aorta.

Progesterone
Maintains uterine lining for implantation of the zygote
Reduces uterine contractions to prevent spontaneous abortion
Prepares the glands of the breasts for lactation

=== SLIDE 61 ===
estrogen
Stimulates uterine growth
Increases blood flow to uterine vessels
Stimulates development of the breast ducts to prepare for lactation
(also: increases skin pigmentation, vascular changes in skin/mucous membranes of nose/mouth, increased salivation)

=== SLIDE 62 ===
hCG is the hormone that signals conception has occurred.
Causes the corpus luteum to persist and continue to produce estrogen and progesterone to sustain the pregnancy
It is detectable in maternal blood as soon as implantation occurs (usually 7 to 9 days after fertilization)

=== SLIDE 65 ===
What does the placenta do?
It removes fetal deoxygenated blood and waste products via the two umbilical arteries, and then oxygenated, nutrient-rich blood from the mother moves through the intervillous space from the spiral arteries in the decidua and returns to the fetus via the umbilical vein.
The thin placental membrane provides some protection but is not a barrier to most substances ingested by the mother.
Many substances such as drugs, nicotine, viral infectious agents are transferred to the fetus, and may cause fetal addiction, congenital anomalies, or fetal infection.

=== SLIDE 66 ===
Umbilical Cord
Lifeline between mother and fetus
Two arteries carry blood away from fetus.
One vein returns blood to the fetus.
Wharton jelly covers and cushions cord vessels.
Normal length is 55 cm (22 inches).
The umbilical cord usually protrudes near the center of the placenta.

=== SLIDE 69 ===
Circulation Before Birth
Blood enters fetal body through umbilical vein.
About half goes to the liver’ remainder enters inferior vena cava through the ductus venosus, goes through foramen ovale, and then ductus arteriosus.
Blood containing waste products is returned to placenta through umbilical arteries.

=== SLIDE 71 ===
Circulation After Birth
Foramen ovale closes within 2 hours after birth (permanently by age 3 months).
Ductus arteriosus closes within 15 hours (permanently in about 3 weeks).
Ductus venosus closes functionally when cord is cut (permanently in about 1 week).
After permanent closure, the ductus arteriosus and ductus venosus become ligaments.

=== SLIDE 78 ===
When do Twins occur?
Monozygotic twins begin to develop at the end of the first week after fertilization.  The result is two identical embryos, each with its own amnion, but with a common chorion and placenta and some common placental vessels. (Mono/mono=monozygotic, monochorionic)
Dizygotic twins: develop from two separate ova fertilized by two separate sperm. Dizygotic twins always have two amnions, two chorions, two placentas, although sometimes the chorions and placentas do fuse. They tend to repeat in families, and twins are about as alike as any other siblings.

=== SLIDE 79 ===
Conjoined twins: This occurs when embryonic disc doesn’t divide completely, form with a single amnion. They are named according to the regions that are joined.
