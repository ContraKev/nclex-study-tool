=== SLIDE 9 ===
Vaginal Changes
Rugae (or vaginal folds) disappear during childbirth, and the vaginal walls become smooth and spacious. They reappear 3 wks postpartum. 
Within 6 wks the vagina has regained most of its pre pregnancy form, but it never returns to the same pre pregnant size.

=== SLIDE 10 ===
Lochia
This is what is comprises the vaginal discharge postpartum, consisting of endometrial tissue, blood and lymph fluids.
Lochia rubra
red, mostly blood, lasts up to 3 days pp
Lochia serosa 
pinkish, more blood/mucus, up to 10th day pp.
Lochia alba 
mostly mucus, clear/colorless/white, lasts from 10th to 21st day postpartum
Assess pads as well as area under pads (underbed) to make sure blood hasn’t overflown beneath patients chux.

=== SLIDE 11 ===
Cervix
The cervix regains muscle tone, but never closes as tightly again.
Some edema persists for a few weeks postpartum
Constant trickle of brighter red lochia is associated with bleeding from lacerations of the cervix, or vagina, especially if the fundus remains firm.

=== SLIDE 12 ===
Resumption of intercourse
Many couples are hesitant to ask
Teach the patient that it is considered safe for intercourse when bleeding has stopped, and the perineum has fully healed (usually 6 weeks)
Lubrication is affected by breastfeeding, is decreased, so K-Y or a contraceptive gel is recommended.
Instruct patient to perform Kegel’s to strengthen muscles involved in urination, bowel function, and vaginal sensations during intercourse.

=== SLIDE 14 ===
Perineum
Perineum is edematous, tender and bruised
Perineum should be assessed for normal healing and signs of complications. 
REEDA: Redness, Edema, Ecchymosis (bruising), Discharge, Approximation
Treatment
Comfort measures: ice pack for the first 12-24 hrs to the perineum
After 24 hours, sitz bath is preferred as it increases circulation and promotes healing.

BUBBLE assessment
Assessment postpartum can follow the BUBBLE acronym.

B: Breasts (soft or engorged)
U: Uterus (observe firmness, fundal height, location)
B: Bladder (observe for bladder distention)
B: Bowels (observe for passage of gas, bowel sounds, stool)
L: Lochia (character, amount, color, odor, clots present)
E: Episiotomy (observe perineum)

This occurs in Chapter 6, as well as here and Chapter 12/13.

Some hospitals now call this the “Golden Hour,” a part of the "Baby Friendly Initiative".

=== SLIDE 50 ===
Cord care
Delayed cord clamping, for 30 to 60 seconds after birth provides better outcomes due to increased iron stores to the newborn.
Cord care is aimed at preventing infection. 
Keep it clean and dry. Fasten diaper beneath to allow air to circulate to the cord.
Teach patients to report redness of area, moist, foul-smelling cord. Sponge bath for first week or until cord stump falls off is a preference. 
Check clamp if parents or nurse reports bleeding from cord postpartum.

Lactation Hormones
Prolactin is the hormone from the anterior pituitary gland and causes production of breast milk
Oxytocin: from the posterior pituitary gland is responsible for “Let Down reflex” (milk ejection) usually is noted by patient as a tingling in her breasts, and sometimes cramping of her uterus/abdominal pains
The only absolute contraindications to breastfeeding are: HIV, and HTLV 1 & 2, all can be transmitted via breast milk to the infant.

=== SLIDE 70 ===
On the physiology of lactation
Let’s review the development of adequate milk supply.
Infant suckling at the breast stimulates the release of oxytocin, causing milk delivery to the nipple.
Prolactin secretion increases as milk is removed from the breasts, which then stimulates further milk production.
Very little milk is stored in between feedings
Composition of the feed changes slightly: 
Foremilk: the first milk the infant obtains.
Hindmilk: later milk, higher fat content, helps satisfy infant’s hunger.
