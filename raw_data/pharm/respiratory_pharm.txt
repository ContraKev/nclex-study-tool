=== SLIDE 5 ===
Antihistamines
Actions
Selectively block the effects of histamine at the H1 receptor sites, decreasing the allergic response.
Inhibits smooth muscle constriction in blood vessels and the respiratory and GI tracts.
Decreases capillary permeability.
Anticholinergic and antipruritic effects.

Indications
Allergic rhinitis, allergic conjunctivitis, uncomplicated urticaria, and angioedema.
Respiratory cold and allergy symptoms.
Motion sickness
Insomnia (1st generation anti-histamines)

=== SLIDE 6 ===
Antihistamines
Examples
1st Generation
Can penetrate the blood-brain barrier and cause CNS symptoms (e.g., drowsiness, dizziness, confusion, urinary retention, dry mouth).
Diphenhydramine (Benadryl), Promethazine, Dimenhydrinate, Brompheniramine (Dimetapp), Chlorpheniramine

2nd Generation
Less potential to penetrate blood-brain barrier.
Cetirizine (Zyrtec), Loratadine (Claritin), Levocetirizine (Xyzal)

3rd Generation
Less potential to penetrate blood-brain barrier.
Lower risk of cardiac toxicity in patients with metabolic issues compared to 2nd generation.
Fexofenadine (Allegra), Desloratadine (Clarinex)

Intranasal forms
Azelastine, Olopatadine

=== SLIDE 7 ===
Antihistamines
Adverse Effects
Anticholinergic symptoms
Cholinergic Effects (muscarinic receptors)
Cholinergic (Ach) = parasympathetic effects
Anticholinergic = opposite of parasympathetic effects
Anticholinergic (antimuscarinic) symptoms
CNS - Headache, confusion, delirium, anxiety, insomnia, impaired memory, and reduced cognitive function. 
PNS - Dry mouth, blurred vision, constipation, urinary retention, tachycardia, flushing, arrhythmias, reduced gut motility, vomiting, reduced saliva, and tear production.
Other - Hyperthermia, anhidrosis, dilated pupils, bowel obstruction, and ejaculatory difficulties.

=== SLIDE 9 ===
Antihistamines
Precautions
Avoid in the elderly due to anticholinergic effects.
Avoid in children – can have paradoxical effect of excitation, (flushed face, fever, tachycardia, pupil dilation, hallucinations, incoordination, and seizures).
Promethazine contraindicated in patients with cardiac dysrhythmias, hepatic diseases, and those on MAOI therapy.

Interactions
CNS depressants/alcohol cause additive CNS depression
Enhanced effect with other medications with anti-cholinergic effects

Intranasal anti-inflammatory agents
Intranasal corticosteroids
Mechanism
Anti-inflammatory.
i.e. inhibits histamine release; suppression of neutrophil chemotaxis; mild vasoconstriction and reduction in edema; and inhibition of mast cell–mediated late-phase reaction.

Indications
Most effective pharmacologic therapy for allergic rhinitis, with persistent or moderate to severe allergic symptoms (sneezing, nasal itching, stuffiness, and rhinorrhea).
Time to effectiveness: 3 – 14d.

=== SLIDE 15 ===
Intranasal anti-inflammatory agents
Chromolyn sodium (NasalChrom)
Mechanism
Mast cell stabilizer that inhibits the release of histamine and other mediators of inflammation.

Indications
Prophylactic – Cromolyn is effective when used shortly (30 minutes) before exposure to allergen inhalation such as cat dander and seasonal allergies.

=== SLIDE 20 ===
decongestants
Action
Sympathomimetic - acts directly on the adrenergic receptors of sympathetic nervous system. 
The drug produces vasoconstriction, which shrinks nasal mucosa membranes, reduce inflammation and opens air passage.

Indications 
Alleviate the stuffiness commonly associated with allergies, sinusitis, colds, and the flu. 

Formulations
Two types of decongestants are systemic decongestants and topical, or nasal, decongestants.
Oxymetazoline nasal spray (Afrin)
Phenylephrine (Sudafed PE), Pseudoephedrine (Sudafed), Naphazoline, Ephedrine

=== SLIDE 21 ===
decongestants
Adverse Effects
Rebound congestion can occur if used for more than 3 days.
Adrenergic effects – CNS stimulation
Nervousness, heart palpitations, and tachycardia 
Restlessness and insomnia
Avoid at bedtime
Avoid caffeine and alcohol
Hypertension due to vasoconstriction
Careful use in patient with heart disease and high blood pressure
Phenylephrine has less impact on blood pressure than pseudoephedrine.
Precautions
Careful use in patients with HTN, hyperthyroidism, diabetes, cardiac disease, increased intraocular pressure (glaucoma), benign prostatic hypertrophy.

=== SLIDE 24 ===
Antitussives (cough suppressants)
Mechanism
It is thought that these medications inhibit the cough center in the brain or soothe nerve receptors around airways to reduce transmission of cough signals to the brain. 
Indication
Treatment of nonproductive cough.
Not recommended for productive coughs where the client is coughing up mucus due to the infection risk that can result from the buildup of mucus in the respiratory system.
Classes
Opioid antitussives
Codeine (typically as combination with guaifenesin or promethazine)
Nonopioid antitussives
Dextromethorphan (Robitussin)
Benzonatate (Tessalon)

=== SLIDE 25 ===
Antitussives (cough suppressants)
Side and Adverse Effect
Opioid antitussives can cause 
CNS effects:  drowsiness, dizziness, confusion
Pulmonary:  respiratory depression
GI:  nausea, vomitting, constipation
Urinary:  retention

Nonopioid antitussives can cause drowsiness, dizziness, headache.
Dextromethorphan has lesser CNS effects.

=== SLIDE 26 ===
Antitussives (cough suppressants)
Precautions
Opioid antitussives can cause respiratory depression. 
Opioid antitussives contraindicated in patients with respiratory depression, acute asthma, head trauma, liver and renal dysfunction, and acute alcohol disorder.
Some OTC formulations of non-opioid antitussives contain alcohol and/or sucrose.
Caution in elderly.
Only provides symptomatic relief and if cough persists (or if accompanied by fever), contact health care provider.

=== SLIDE 29 ===
expectorants
Mechanism
Expectorants work by lubricating the airways, stimulating the cough reflex, and decreasing mucus viscosity so that it is easier to expel.
Indications
Used with productive coughs when it is necessary to loosen and clear mucus.
Guaifenesin (Mucinex)
Guaifenesin is a common expectorant and is available over the counter without a prescription.  
Can be combined with antitussive or decongestant formulation.
Adverse effects – Typically does not cause many adverse effects, but dizziness, headache, nausea, and vomiting may occur. 
Can cause bronchospasm in those with asthma.
Normal Saline Solution
NS solutions are typically administered via nebulization to hydrate mucus and reduce viscosity. 
Adverse effects – none.

=== SLIDE 30 ===
mucolytics
Mechanism
Clear mucus from the airway by breaking its protein bonds, helping to reduce the thickness and stickiness of mucus. 

Indications
They differ from expectorants in that they are used to help high-risk respiratory clients cough up thick, tenacious mucus. 
Mucus is a breeding ground for bacteria.
Patients with tracheostomies often benefit from mucolytics.
Typically inhaled.

=== SLIDE 31 ===
mucolytics
Acetylcysteine
Used in clients with pneumonia, bronchitis, cystic fibrosis, and emphysema. 
Inhalation used prior to bronchial studies (e.g., bronchoscopy) to help clear the airway and is also used as routine tracheostomy care. 
Acetylcysteine has a sulfur content that can cause a rotten egg odor.
Acetylcysteine is also administered orally or intravenously as the antidote for acetaminophen toxicity.

Dornase Alfa
Inhaled via nebulizer
Cystic fibrosis. Dornase alfa acts like an enzyme naturally found in the lungs to help thin out thick, sticky mucus.

=== SLIDE 32 ===
mucolytics
Adverse Effects
Dizziness, drowsiness
Tachycardia
Hypotension
Nausea, and vomiting
Voice changes, hoarseness, and sore throat
Pharyngitis
Bronchospasm
Aspiration (secondary to vomiting)

Contraindications
Acetylcysteine commonly causes vomiting – avoid in patients with peptic ulcer disease, esophageal varices.

=== SLIDE 37 ===
Lower respiratory conditions
Two types: obstructive and restrictive. 

Obstructive airway diseases are those that narrow air passages, create turbulence, and increase resistance to airflow. 
Symptoms – smooth muscle constriction (bronchospasm), edema, inflammation of the bronchial walls, or excess mucus secretion. 
Examples – COPD (emphysema, chronic bronchitis); asthma, acute bronchitis

Restrictive airway diseases are those in which lung expansion is limited from loss of elasticity.
Examples – Pulmonary fibrosis (interstitial lung disease), sarcoidosis, physical deformity of the chest (e.g., kyphosis).

=== SLIDE 44 ===
Beta2 selective adrenergic agonists
Mechanism
Stimulating the beta-2 adrenergic receptors, relaxing the smooth muscles in the airways and leading to bronchodilation and improved airflow.
Inhibition of histamine release.

Indication
Bronchospasm (as in asthma, bronchitis, exercise-induced)

=== SLIDE 45 ===
Beta2 selective adrenergic agonists
Short acting Meds
Onset: 1-2 min
Frequency: q3-4h
Example:  Albuterol (Proventil, Ventolin, Accuneb), Levalbuterol (Xopenex)

Long acting Meds
Example:  Salmeterol (Severent) – onset 10-15 min; q12h dosing
Example:  Formoterol – onset 5-15 min; q24h dosing
Note: Due to concern that prolonged use of long acting beta-2 agonists can make acute episodes worse, an asthma controller medication (e.g., inhaled corticosteroid) should be used prior to using a long acting Beta-2 agonist; and both can be used together.

=== SLIDE 46 ===
Beta2 selective adrenergic agonists
Adverse Effects and Contraindications
Common adverse effects are due to adrenergic activity 
Cardiac – tachycardia, palpitations, hypertension
CNS – tremors, nervousness, anxiety, restlessness, headache
GI – nausea, vomiting

Precautions
Clients with severe cardiac disease should use albuterol only at the direction of their health care provider due to systemic effects on heart rate and blood pressure.

Drug interactions
Anti-arhythmics – can affect efficacy
Nonselective beta blockers – reduce effectiveness
CNS stimulants – can have additive effects

Anticholinergic bronchodilating agents
Mechanism
Competitively bind to cholinergic receptors on bronchial smooth muscle, blocking the bronchoconstriction action of vagal efferent impulses. 
Indication
Bronchoconstriction associated with asthma, chronic bronchitis, and emphysema.
Short Acting
Ipatropium – q6h
Long Acting
Aclidinium, glycopyrrolate – q12h
Revefenacin, tiotropium, umeclidinium – q24h
Meta-analysis suggests that anticholinergics are superior to beta2 agonists in reducing severe exacerbations and respiratory deaths.
Titotropium capsule to be used with inh device

Mechanism
Anti-inflammatory effect including direct effect on smooth muscle relaxation.
Indication
Used for maintenance therapy of bronchoconstriction associated with asthma and COPD.
Formulations

Also comes in combination formulations

Leukotriene receptorantagonist
Mechanism
Blocks leukotriene receptors and decreases inflammation.

Indication
Use in conjunction with other medications for the prophylactic and chronic treatment of asthma.
Can be used as alternatives to low-dose inhaled corticosteroids for mild persistent asthma or use with low to medium doses of inhaled corticosteroids for moderate persistent asthma.

Drugs
Montelukast (Singulair)
Zafirlukast (Accolate)
Zileuton

=== SLIDE 57 ===
Leukotriene receptorantagonist
Adverse Effects
Headaches, dizziness, epistaxis, urticaria
Serious neuropsychiatric events (aggressive behavior, agitation, depression and suicidality) have been reported in clients taking montelukast. 
Reconsider use in mild asthma or exercise-induced bronchospasm given psychiatric risks.
Hepatotoxicity with zileuton and zafirlukast

Interactions
Zileuton and zafirlukast  increases warfarin levels 
Phenytoin decrease effectiveness of montelukast

=== SLIDE 58 ===
Xanthines
Mechanism
Relaxes bronchial smooth muscle by nonselective inhibition of the enzyme phosphodiesterase and suppresses airway responsiveness to stimuli that cause bronchoconstriction.

Indication
Long-term management of persistent asthma that is unresponsive to beta agonists or inhaled corticosteroids.
Medication has fallen out of favor due to narrow therapeutic range (5-15 mcg/mL) and side effects.
Needs periodic monitoring of blood levels

Drugs
Theophylline 
Aminophylline

=== SLIDE 59 ===
XanthinEs
Adverse Effects
Xanthine toxicity
GI:  Nausea, vomiting are early signs.
CNS:  Stimulation, nervousness, confusion and insomnia.
Severe:  Dysrhythmias and seizures.

Precautions/Interactions
Avoid CNS stimulants, including caffeine (which can also increase theophylline levels).
Cimetidine, ciprofloxacin and other fluoroquinolone antibiotics increase theophylline levels.
Tobacco and marijuana can decrease theophylline levels.
Phenobarbital, phenytoin, and rifampin decrease theophylline levels.

=== SLIDE 60 ===
Inhaled Mast cell stabilizer
Drug: Cromolyn sodium (Intal)

Actions: Mast cell stabilizer; inhibits release of histamines and other mediators of inflammation.

Use: In combination with other agents to treat severe bronchial asthma or allergic rhinitis.
Common adverse effects:  Oral irritation, dry mouth.

Serious adverse effects:  Bronchospasm, coughing.

=== SLIDE 61 ===
Phosphodiesterase-4 inhibitor: roflumilast
Action: Block inflammation (selective phosphodiesterase-4 inhibitor)

Formulation:  po

Use: Reduce frequency of flare-ups of inflammation in patients with chronic bronchitis, severe COPD; used with bronchodilators and corticosteroids.
Common adverse effects:  Headache and mainly GI (Dyspepsia, cramps, nausea, diarrhea, weight loss).

Severe adverse effects:  Psychiatric (Insomnia, anxiety, depression, suicidal thoughts, mood changes).

=== SLIDE 62 ===
Immunomodulator agents
Drug: Omalizumab (Xolair), benralizumab.

Action:  Humanized monoclonal antibodies bind to circulating IgE antibodies, inhibiting mast cell release of inflammatory chemicals.

Use: Treatment of moderate to severe persistent allergic asthma that have failed other therapies.
Common and serious adverse effects:  Hypersensitivity, injection site reactions.
