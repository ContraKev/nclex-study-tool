=== SLIDE 2 ===
TIPS FOR LEARNING PHARMACOLOGY
Learn in small chunks.  Too much information at one time is overwhelming.  Over time, you will be able to add more information.

For each drug, learn one or two items from each category such as adverse effects, interactions, etc.  Typically, they test you on something that can cause an emergency or something “unusual” or “unique” about the drug.  
They assume you already know the routine stuff.

=== SLIDE 3 ===
TIPS FOR LEARNING PHARMACOLOGY
When they give you a scenario, they are not going to state that the patient has, for instance, “nephrotoxicity.”  Instead, they will say something like “the client has decreased urine output (<30 mL/h), hematuria, increased serum BUN and creatinine” which indicates there is something wrong with the kidneys.  You have to recognize that the kidney is being affected by a drug.

Try to find some commonalities among drugs.  For instance, group drugs that cause ototoxicity  or drugs that can increase or decrease the effectiveness of warfarin (an anticoagulant).  
Make your own tables or outlines of drugs.

=== SLIDE 4 ===
TIPS FOR LEARNING PHARMACOLOGY
Look things up and keep looking things up if you don’t remember or understand a term.  That is how you recall memory.

Do a lot of ATI questions.  Understand why the answer is correct as well as why the other answers are incorrect.  Over time you will learn that there is a pattern as to how questions and answer choices (both correct and incorrect ones) are made.

3 Factors in the Selection of Antibiotics
Identity of the type of bacteria.

Which antibiotic is most effective against this type of bacteria.

*Empirical treatment 
Prescribing medication based on the anticipated cause of an infection or illness, rather than a specific identification of the causative agent. 
Used when immediate action is needed before precise diagnostic tests (like blood cultures) are available.

=== SLIDE 14 ===
Broad-spectrum vs Narrow-spectrum antibiotics
Narrow-spectrum targets either gram-positive or gram-negative bacteria.

Broad-spectrum is effective against both gram-negative and gram-positive bacteria.

Broad-spectrum antibiotics impact the individual’s microbiome and can cause diarrhea and secondary infections.

=== SLIDE 15 ===

Bacteriostatic relies on immune system to eliminate infection.

=== SLIDE 16 ===
Host Factors
Immune system
People with a compromised immune system need a strong bactericidal antibiotics, not bacteriostatic medication.
Site of infection
Some sites are difficult for antimicrobials to reach (e.g., brain, CSF, bone, thrombus, abscess or prosthesis).
Age
Infants and older adults are at increased risk for toxicity due to slower metabolism and excretion of medication.

=== SLIDE 17 ===
Host Factors
Presence of previous allergic reaction
Especially with penicillin.
Note:  Allergic reactions can range from severe anaphylaxis to a rash discomfort.

Pregnancy and Lactation
Some medications are teratogenic.
Tetracyclines cause discoloration of developing teeth.
Gentamicin causes hearing loss in infants.
Sulfonamides can produce kernicterus, a severe neurologic disorder, in newborns.
Lactation will transmit drugs to breast feeding infant.

=== SLIDE 27 ===
Antibiotics Affecting the Bacterial Cell Wall:  Penicillins(“-cillin”)
Therapeutic Uses
Primarily infections due to gram-positive cocci (Streptococcus).

Meningitis due to gram-negative cocci (Neisseria or meningococci).

Syphillis (Treponema pallidum).

Extended-spectrum penicillins (piperacillin, ticarcillin) are effective against a broad range of organisms (Pseudomonas, Enterobacter species, Proteus, Klebsiella).

=== SLIDE 28 ===

Allergic reactions from penicillin allergy.

GI: Nausea, vomiting and diarrhea – “big three” adverse effects of all antimicrobials
Diarrhea may signify suprainfection with Clostridium Difficile colitis.
Nausea and vomiting may not require discontinuation of medication.

Nephrotoxicity/nephritis.

Penicillin G (high dosages) – hyperkalemia, dysrhythmias

=== SLIDE 31 ===
Antibiotics Affecting the Bacterial Cell Wall:  Cephalosporins(“Cef-”)
Therapeutic Uses

First-generation cephalosporins are effective mainly against gram-positive organisms (i.e. staph and strep). 

Higher generations generally have expanded spectra against aerobic gram-negative bacilli. 

Later generations can reach CSF and are broader spectrum, effective against Gram-negative and anaerobic organisms.

5th generation Ceftaroline effective against MRSA.

=== SLIDE 32 ===
Antibiotics Affecting the Bacterial Cell Wall:  Cephalosporins(“Cef-”)
Complications
Allergy:  Potential for cross-sensitivity for those with penicillin allergies.  

Secondary infections:  Broad-spectrum antibiotics have increased risk of C. difficile pseudomembranous colitis.

Bleeding tendencies:  Increased bleeding risks for those with bleeding disorders or on anticoagulants (from cefotetan and ceftriaxone).

Renal impairments:  Most cephalosporins are eliminated by the kidney.  Ceftriaxone is the exception where it is mainly eliminated by the liver.

=== SLIDE 33 ===

Avoid concurrent use with alcohol – cefotetan can cause disulfiram reaction.

No alcohol is basically the advice for taking all antibiotics and may resume 48h after completion of course.

=== SLIDE 37 ===
Antibiotics Affecting the Bacterial Cell Wall:  Others
Vancomycin Adverse Effects

Ototoxicity (vertigo, tinnitus, hearing loss)
Increased risk with other meds that can cause ototoxicity (loop diuretics, aminoglycoside antibiotics, amphotericin B).

Nephrotoxicity.  (Decreased urine output <30 mL/h; elevated Cr/BUN; hematuria).

=== SLIDE 49 ===
Antibiotics Affecting Protein Synthesis:Tetracycline (“-cycline”)
Medications:  Tetracycline, doxyclycline, minocycline, demeclocycline
Broad-spectrum, bacteriostatic.

Complications
GI discomfort:  nausea, vomiting, diarrhea and cramping, esophageal ulceration (avoid at bedtime or avoid lying down for at least 30 min).

Dental:  yellow or brown teeth discoloration, and hypoplasia of tooth enamel.

Hepatoxicity:  lethargy, jaundice, elevated LFTs.

Photosensitivity:  intense sunburn can be avoided with protective clothing and sunscreen.

Suprainfection:  C. Difficile, yeast infections.

=== SLIDE 50 ===
Antibiotics Affecting Protein Synthesis:Tetracycline (“-cycline”)
Precautions
Decrease effectiveness of oral contraceptives.
Hepatoxic and renal toxic, although doxycycline and minocycline are generally safe for renal disease.

Interactions
Take with water, not food.  
Milk products, calcium and iron supplements, laxatives containing magnesium and antacids reduce absorption of tetracyclines.  Can take these items 1h before or 2h after meals.

=== SLIDE 51 ===
Antibiotics Affecting Protein Synthesis:Macrolides (“-thromycin”)
Medications:  Erythromycin, clarithromycin, azithromycin
Bacteriostatic
Alternative for penicillin allergy

Complications
GI discomfort:  nausea, vomiting, diarrhea and epigastric pain.
Typically taken on an empty stomach but can be taken with food.

Cardiovascular:  prolongs QT intervals, dysrhythmias, cardiac arrest.




Ototoxicity:  hearing loss, tinnitus, vertigo particularly with high dosages.

Hepatotoxicity:  fatigue, jaundice, elevated LFTs.

=== SLIDE 52 ===
Antibiotics Affecting Protein Synthesis:Macrolides (“-thromycin”)
Interactions
Increases blood levels of carbamezapine, warfarin, and digoxin.

Erythromycin levels increased by verapamil, diltiazem, HIV protease inhibitors, antifungal medications, and nefazodone (antidepressant).  
Toxic levels of erythromycin can lead to more severe complications including dysrhythmias (e.g. prolonged QT interval) and cardiac arrest.

=== SLIDE 110 ===
Anti-Virals
Most antivirals work by inhibiting a process in replicating viral particle

Depending on the infection, antiviral drugs can be:
Curative
Reduce symptoms
Preventative

Antivirals can prevent the spread of viral infections after exposure and reduce risk of spreading to others.  For example, taking antivirals:

Within 48 hours (two days) of exposure to the flu may keep you from getting sick.

Within 72 hours (three days) of a potential HIV exposure can lower the chances you get infected.

Every day can lower the risk of passing herpes or HIV to others or getting them from a sexual partner who has it.

During pregnancy lowers the risk of a pregnant person passing a virus they have to the fetus (babies will also receive antivirals after they’re born).
