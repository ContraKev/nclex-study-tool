=== SLIDE 10 ===
Nursing Pain Assessment
Considered fifth vital sign
If pain is not controlled, nursing functions and outcome will be impaired.
Assess pain before and after interventions
The nurse should also evaluate the pain level immediately before and after pain medications are given; at 1-, 2-, and 3-hour intervals for oral medications; and at 15- to 30-minute intervals after parenteral administration.

=== SLIDE 13 ===
Nursing evaluation
Always evaluate the patient’s response to pain treatments
With analgesic medications, the nurse should assess for decrease in pain 30 minutes after IV administration and 60 minutes after oral medication. 

Evaluate for any adverse effects (e.g., check respiratory rate, O2 sat since respiratory depression can occur with opioid analgesics).

The nurse may need to consider administering other medications (e.g., anti-emetics medications) that treat the side effects of analgesic medication.

=== SLIDE 14 ===

Provide patient/family teaching

Record amount used and degree of pain relief

=== SLIDE 23 ===
Drug Class: ACETAMINOPHEN (Tylenol)
Side/Adverse Effects
Nausea and vomitting
Hepatoxic
Long-term, high dosage use may lead to bone marrow issues – anemia, leukopenia, thombocytopenia.  (Rare.)

Precautions/Interactions
Use caution in clients with alcohol use (~3-4 beverages a day)
Consider use with concurrent use of hepatoxic medications (rifampin, INH, carbamezapine, and barbituates)

Nursing Interventions and Client Education
Monitor liver function
Be aware of OTC sources of acetaminophen
Take as prescribed, and do not exceed 3000mg/d
Treat acetaminophen overdose with acetylcysteine

=== SLIDE 48 ===
Drug Class: Opiate Agonists
Fentanyl
Indication for severe pain.
Available as parenteral injections, transdermal patch (Duragesic), buccal lozenges (Fentora), and buccal lozenges on a stick (Actiq).
Transdermal formulation often used for chronic pain in non-opioid-naïve patients.  
Patch is applied every 72h.  It takes 6-12 hours to reach steady state so oral opioids should not be discontinued during this period.

Methadone (Dolophine)
Drug of choice for detoxification treatment of opioid addicts
Also used for chronic and cancer related pain
Has longer half-life and drug can accumulate in tissues which allows for qd (or q12h) dosing.
Eliminated by liver so can be used with renal insufficiency.
Can cause cardiac dysrhythmia.

=== SLIDE 57 ===
Drug Class: Opiate PARTIAL Agonist
Indications
Short term pain relief (up to 3 weeks) in opioid-naïve patients of moderate to severe pain
Potency similar to morphine.
“Ceiling effect” – at a particular dosage, a larger dose does not produce a significantly greater analgesic effect.

Buprenorphine is also used for opioid use disorder
Helps reduce cravings, prevent withdrawal symptoms, and block the euphoric effects of other opioids, thereby promoting recovery and reducing harmful behaviors associated with opioid misuse.

Nalbuphine has minimal physical dependence liability and is not a controlled substance. 
Because nalbuphine has a ceiling effect for analgesia and respiratory depression, it is often used as an analgesic for obstetric patients.

=== SLIDE 73 ===
Takeaways
Pain control is a nursing priority.

Believe the patient when they say they have pain.

Assess pain before and after treatment.  Monitor for adverse effects.  Evaluate at periodic intervals that pain treatments are effective.

Nursing functions and patient outcome will be impaired if pain is not controlled.
For example, you will not be able to ambulate the patient if he has pain.

=== SLIDE 76 ===
Takeaways
Opioid Agonists
Watch out for different potencies of opioids.
Watch out for respiratory depression, hypotension, bradycardia.
Watch out for confusion, sedation, altered mental status.
Typical side effects include N/V, pruritus, sweating, constipation, urinary retention (watch out for this too).

Opioid Partial Agonists
Similar side effects as agonists but typically less so.
Watch out for withdrawal (abstinence) syndrome in patients who are dependent on opioids.
Buprenorphine is used to treat opioid use disorder.

Opioid Antagonists
Used to reverse effects of opioids.
Can precipitate withdrawal (abstinence) syndrome.
