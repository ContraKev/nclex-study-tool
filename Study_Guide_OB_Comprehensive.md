## Chapter 3: Fetal Development

### 1. Cell Biology Foundations

Before a baby can develop, you need to understand how cells work at the most basic level.

**Mitosis vs. Meiosis**
- **Mitosis** is how the body grows and repairs itself. One cell splits into two identical copies, each with the full set of 46 chromosomes (23 pairs). This is what happens in ordinary tissue growth — skin healing, organs growing, etc.
- **Meiosis** is special cell division that only happens in the sex cells (sperm and ova). It reduces the chromosome count in half, to 23. This way, when a sperm (23 chromosomes) fertilizes an ovum (23 chromosomes), the resulting zygote has the normal 46.

**Why it matters clinically**: Errors during meiosis cause chromosomal disorders. Trisomy 21 (Down syndrome) occurs when chromosome 21 fails to separate properly, giving the offspring three copies instead of two. Turner syndrome involves a missing or incomplete X chromosome.

### 2. Gametogenesis and Fertilization

**Gametogenesis** is the process of forming sex cells:
- In males: **spermatogenesis** (continuous from puberty)
- In females: **oogenesis** (begins at ovulation, completes at fertilization). One oocyte matures per menstrual cycle.

**The Moment of Fertilization**:
- Occurs in the **outer third (ampulla) of the fallopian tube**
- Sperm can reach the fallopian tubes within **5 minutes** of ejaculation
- Sperm remain viable for **up to 5 days**
- The ovum is viable for only **24 hours** after release
- Sex is determined at fertilization: sperm carrying an X chromosome → XX (female), Y chromosome → XY (male)
- The union of sperm and ovum creates a **zygote** — the very first cell of a new organism

**Inheritance**:
- Genes carry dominant and recessive traits
- If one parent carries a dominant trait, ~50% of offspring will show it
- If both parents carry a recessive gene, there is a 25% chance a child will express that recessive trait

### 3. From Fertilization to Implantation

This journey takes approximately **one week**:

1. **Zygote** — the single fertilized cell begins rapid mitotic division (called **cleavage**)
2. **Morula** — by about day 3, the dividing cells form a solid ball of 16-32 cells that enters the uterus
3. **Blastocyst** — the morula develops a cavity and two cell layers (inner and outer). It floats in the uterus for 2-4 more days
4. **Implantation** — the blastocyst burrows into the endometrium, usually in the **upper portion of the posterior uterine wall**

Once implantation occurs, the endometrium is called the **decidua** (because of greater vascularization). The area directly under the blastocyst is called the **decidua basalis**, which becomes the **maternal part of the placenta**.

**Key point**: During the week between fertilization and implantation, all cells within the zygote are identical. After implantation, cells begin to **differentiate** and develop specialized functions. Four structures appear: the chorion, amnion, yolk sac, and primary germ layers.

### 4. The Chorion and Amnion

These two membranes are critical structures:
- **Chorion**: develops from the outer layer (trophoblast); envelops the amnion, embryo, and yolk sac. Eventually forms the **fetal portion of the placenta**.
- **Amnion**: thin inner membrane that directly envelops the embryo
- Together they form the **amniotic sac**

**Amniotic fluid volume** increases throughout pregnancy:
- ~30 mL at 10 weeks
- ~350 mL at 20 weeks
- ~**1000 mL at 37 weeks**

Too little fluid = **oligohydramnios**. Too much = **polyhydramnios** (hydramnios).

**Functions of amniotic fluid**: cushions the fetus, maintains temperature, allows movement for musculoskeletal development, prevents adhesions.

### 5. The Three Germ Layers

After implantation, three primary germ layers form that give rise to ALL body tissues:

**Ectoderm** (outer layer):
- Outer layer of skin, oil glands, hair follicles
- Nails and hair
- External sense organs (eyes, ears)
- Mucous membrane of mouth and anus
- **The entire nervous system**

**Mesoderm** (middle layer):
- True skin (dermis)
- Skeleton, bone and cartilage
- Connective tissue
- Muscles
- Blood and blood vessels
- Kidneys and gonads

**Endoderm** (inner layer):
- Lining of trachea, pharynx, and bronchi
- Lining of the digestive tract
- Lining of bladder and urethra

**Memory trick**: Ecto = Exterior (skin + nerves). Meso = Middle/Muscle. Endo = Internal linings.

### 6. Embryonic and Fetal Milestones

- By the end of **week 8**: ALL body systems are formed and functioning in a simple way. The embryo has a distinct human appearance, the tail has disappeared, sex organs are forming. Crown-rump length is ~30mm, weight ~6 grams. The embryo now enters the **fetal period**.
- **20 weeks**: **age of viability** (per textbook) — the fetus could theoretically survive outside the uterus, though clinically viability is closer to 22-24 weeks. Fewer than 40% of infants born at 23-25 weeks survive.
- **37 weeks**: considered term for obstetrical purposes
- **39-40 weeks 6 days**: full term

### 7. The Placenta

The placenta is a **temporary organ** that serves as the site for fetal respiration, nutrition, and excretion. It also acts as an **endocrine gland**, producing **four hormones**:

| Hormone | Key Functions |
|---------|--------------|
| **Progesterone** | Maintains uterine lining for implantation; reduces uterine contractions (prevents spontaneous abortion); prepares breast glands for lactation; stimulates fetal testes to produce testosterone |
| **Estrogen** | Stimulates uterine growth; increases blood flow to uterine vessels; stimulates breast duct development; increases skin pigmentation; vascular changes in skin and mucous membranes |
| **hCG (human chorionic gonadotropin)** | Signals that conception has occurred; causes corpus luteum to persist and continue producing estrogen and progesterone; detectable in blood 7-9 days after fertilization (this is what pregnancy tests detect) |
| **hPL (human placental lactogen)** | Decreases maternal insulin sensitivity and glucose utilization, making more glucose available to the fetus for growth ("diabetogenic effect"); also called human chorionic somatomammotropin |

**What the placenta does NOT do**: The thin placental membrane allows exchange of oxygen and waste products without mixing maternal and fetal blood. However, it is **not a barrier** — most substances ingested by the mother (drugs, nicotine, viral agents) can cross to the fetus.

### 8. The Umbilical Cord

- Contains **2 arteries and 1 vein** (remember: "AVA" — Artery, Vein, Artery)
- **Arteries** carry deoxygenated blood and waste products **away** from the fetus to the placenta
- **Vein** carries oxygenated, nutrient-rich blood **to** the fetus
- Covered by **Wharton jelly** — a gelatinous substance that cushions the vessels and keeps them separated, preventing compression
- Normal length: **55 cm (22 inches)**
- Usually protrudes near the center of the placenta

### 9. Fetal Circulation and the Three Shunts

Fetal circulation is unique because the lungs and liver are not yet fully functional. Three shunts redirect blood flow:

**1. Ductus Venosus** — diverts some blood **away from the liver** as it returns from the placenta via the umbilical vein
- Closes functionally when the cord is cut
- Closes permanently in about 1 week → becomes a ligament

**2. Foramen Ovale** — an opening between the right and left atria that diverts most blood from the **right atrium directly to the left atrium**, bypassing the lungs
- Closes functionally within **2 hours** after birth
- Closes permanently by about **3 months** of age

**3. Ductus Arteriosus** — diverts most blood from the **pulmonary artery into the aorta**, also bypassing the lungs
- Closes functionally within **15 hours** after birth
- Closes permanently in about **3 weeks** → becomes a ligament
- Failure to close = **patent ductus arteriosus (PDA)**, a congenital heart defect

**After birth**: The baby's first breath expands the lungs, dramatically increasing pulmonary blood flow. This triggers closure of all three shunts. The ductus arteriosus and ductus venosus become ligaments after permanent closure.

### 10. Twins and Multifetal Pregnancy

**Monozygotic (identical) twins**: develop from a single fertilized ovum that splits at the end of the first week. They share a common chorion and placenta with some common vessels. Each has its own amnion.

**Dizygotic (fraternal) twins**: develop from two separate ova fertilized by two separate sperm. They always have two amnions, two chorions, and two placentas (though chorions/placentas may fuse). They are genetically as similar as any two siblings. Tend to run in families.

**Conjoined twins**: occur when the embryonic disc does not divide completely. They form within a single amnion and are named according to the regions that are joined.

### 11. Teratogens and Impaired Development

**Teratogens** are agents that cause damage to growing embryonic cells, potentially resulting in malformation. Examples include:
- Certain prescribed medications
- Alcohol, tobacco, cocaine, heroin
- Maternal undernutrition
- Infections (TORCH)
- Environmental chemicals and radiation

**Undernutrition in utero** can cause permanent changes in fetal structure, physiology, and metabolism, potentially leading to chronic conditions later in life. This is why maternal nutrition during pregnancy is so critical.

## Chapter 4: Prenatal Care and Adaptations to Pregnancy

### 1. Obstetric Terminology

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Gravida** | Total number of pregnancies (including current) |
| **Para** | Number of pregnancies carried to ≥20 weeks (regardless of whether infant was born alive or still) |
| **Nulligravida** | A woman who has never been pregnant |
| **Primigravida** | A woman in her first pregnancy |
| **Multigravida** | A woman who has been pregnant more than once |
| **Nullipara** | A woman who has never given birth past 20 weeks |
| **Primipara** | A woman who has given birth once past 20 weeks |
| **Multipara** | A woman who has given birth 2 or more times past 20 weeks |
| **GTPAL** | Gravida, Term births, Preterm births, Abortions/miscarriages, Living children |
| **LNMP/LMP** | Last normal menstrual period |
| **Quickening** | First fetal movements felt by the mother |
| **Lightening** | When the fetus drops into the pelvis near term |
| **Postpartum** | Period after delivery |

### 2. Phases and Goals of Prenatal Care

Pregnancy has three phases:
- **Antepartum** (before delivery)
- **Intrapartum** (during labor and delivery)
- **Postpartum** (after delivery)

**Preconceptual care**: Care provided when planning a pregnancy. Identifies current medical issues, medications, and lifestyle concerns to optimize health before conception.

**Major goals of prenatal care**:
1. Ensure a healthy outcome for mother and baby
2. Identify risk factors early
3. Provide education and support
4. Monitor fetal growth and development
5. Prevent or manage complications

### 3. Determining the Due Date

**Nägele's Rule**:
1. Identify the first day of the last menstrual period (LMP)
2. Count backward 3 months
3. Add 7 days
4. Update the year if applicable

**Example**: LMP = May 5, 2025 → Back 3 months = February 5 → Add 7 days = **February 12, 2026**

Average pregnancy = **40 weeks (280 days)** from the first day of LMP, ±2 weeks.

**Trimesters**:
- First: weeks 1-12
- Second: weeks 13-27
- Third: weeks 28-40

### 4. Signs of Pregnancy

**Presumptive signs** (subjective — felt by the woman, could have other causes):
- Amenorrhea (missed period)
- Nausea and vomiting ("morning sickness")
- Breast tenderness and changes
- Urinary frequency
- Fatigue and drowsiness
- Quickening (fetal movement felt by mother, ~16-20 weeks)
- Deepening skin pigmentation

**Probable signs** (objective — detected by examiner, but still not 100% proof):
- **Goodell sign**: softening of the cervix
- **Chadwick sign**: purplish/bluish color of the cervix and vagina (increased blood supply)
- **Hegar sign**: softening of the lower uterine segment
- **McDonald sign**: ease of flexing the uterus on the cervix
- Abdominal enlargement
- Braxton Hicks contractions (irregular, painless "practice" contractions)
- Ballottement (rebounding of fetus when tapped through abdomen)
- Abdominal striae (stretch marks)
- **Positive pregnancy test** (detects hCG — still only "probable" because rare conditions like molar pregnancy can also elevate hCG)

**Positive signs** (definitive — only explained by pregnancy):
- Fetal heartbeat detected by examiner (Doppler ~10-12 weeks, fetoscope ~18-20 weeks)
- Fetal movements felt by examiner (not mother)
- Visualization of fetus on ultrasound

### 5. Prenatal Testing by Trimester

**First Trimester**:
- Blood type and Rh factor with antibody screen
- CBC (complete blood count)
- RPR/VDRL (syphilis screening)
- Rubella titer (immunity check)
- TB screening (PPD or QuantiFERON-TB Gold)
- Hepatitis B surface antigen
- HIV screening
- Urinalysis and urine culture
- Pap test
- Vaginal cultures for chlamydia and gonorrhea
- Hemoglobin A1C
- First trimester genetic screening (NIPT, CF/genetic markers)
- Optional: hemoglobin electrophoresis (sickle cell), transvaginal ultrasound

**Second Trimester**:
- Quad screen (AFP, hCG, estriol, inhibin-A) — screens for Down syndrome, neural tube defects
- Anatomy ultrasound (~18-22 weeks)
- Glucose challenge test for gestational diabetes screening (**24-28 weeks**)

**Third Trimester**:
- Real-time ultrasound and Doppler blood flow
- **GBS (Group B Strep) culture** at **35-37 weeks**
- Cervical fetal fibronectin (preterm labor risk)
- Possible repeat STI/HIV testing, CBC, RPR
- NST (non-stress test), BPP (biophysical profile), AFI (amniotic fluid index)

### 6. Routine Assessments at Every Prenatal Visit

At each visit, the nurse should assess:
1. **Risk factors** (known and newly identified)
2. **Vital signs and weight** (is weight gain normal?)
3. **Urinalysis** (checking for glucose and protein)
4. **Fundal height** (to assess fetal growth — should roughly equal gestational age in cm from 20-36 weeks)
5. **Leopold maneuvers** (assessing fetal presentation and position in late pregnancy)
6. **Fetal heart rate** (normal: 110-160 bpm)
7. **Nutrition assessment** (adequate intake of key nutrients?)
8. **Discomforts or problems** since last visit
9. Any ER visits or hospitalizations

### 7. Physiological Changes During Pregnancy

**Uterine changes**: The uterus grows from ~60g to ~1000g. Blood supply increases dramatically.

**Vaginal changes**: Increased blood supply, thickened mucosa, more prominent rugae. Secretions increase. pH becomes more acidic (protective against some infections but increases risk for yeast infections).

**Breast changes**: Hormone-induced changes begin early. High estrogen and progesterone prepare for lactation. Areolae darken, sebaceous glands become prominent, colostrum may be expressed in the last few months.

**Cardiovascular changes**: Blood volume increases 40-50%. Heart rate increases. Cardiac output increases. Blood pressure may decrease slightly in second trimester.

**Supine Hypotension Syndrome**: When a pregnant woman lies flat on her back, the heavy uterus compresses the **inferior vena cava**, reducing blood return to the heart. This causes a sudden drop in blood pressure, dizziness, and pallor.
- **Nursing intervention: Turn the patient to her LEFT SIDE** (displaces the uterus off the vena cava)
- This is especially important during fetal monitoring, labor, and anytime the patient is supine

**Hematologic changes** — pregnancy blood values differ from non-pregnant:
| Lab Value | Pregnant | Non-Pregnant |
|-----------|----------|-------------|
| Hemoglobin | 11-12 g/dL | 12-16 g/dL |
| Hematocrit | 33-46% | 36-48% |
| RBC | 4.5-6.5 million/mm3 | 3.8-5.1 million |
| WBC | 5,000-15,000/mm3 | 5,000-10,000 |
| Fibrinogen | 300-600 mg/dL | 200-400 |

Note: The lower hemoglobin/hematocrit is called **physiologic anemia of pregnancy** — it occurs because plasma volume increases more than red blood cell volume.

### 8. Nutrition in Pregnancy

There is a **high correlation between maternal diet and fetal health**. Four nutrients are especially critical:

**Protein** (60 g/day): Needed for metabolism and growth/repair of maternal and fetal tissues. Best sources: meat, fish, poultry, dairy products.

**Calcium** (1000 mg DRI): Essential for fetal skeletal development. Requires adequate Vitamin D for absorption. Sources: dairy, enriched cereals, legumes, nuts, dried fruits, broccoli, leafy greens, canned salmon/sardines.

**Iron** (30 mg/day — double the non-pregnant requirement): Prevents iron-deficiency anemia. **Ferrous sulfate** should be taken with **orange juice or vitamin C** to enhance absorption. Do NOT take with milk, antacids, or calcium (they inhibit absorption). Side effects include dark/black stools and constipation.

**Folic acid** (400 mcg pre-conception through early pregnancy, then 600-800 mcg/day): A water-soluble B vitamin that significantly reduces the incidence of **neural tube defects** (spina bifida, anencephaly). Should ideally be started before conception.

**Important warnings**:
- **Excess Vitamin A is teratogenic** — can cause fetal anomalies and cardiac defects
- **Vitamin B6 (pyridoxine) and ginger** are recommended for pregnancy nausea
- **PICA**: the craving and ingestion of non-food substances (clay, starch, raw flour, cracked ice). It can indicate nutritional deficiencies and may be harmful.

**Normal weight gain**: Approximately 25-35 lbs for a woman with normal pre-pregnancy BMI.

### 9. Exercise and Activity During Pregnancy

**Safe exercises**: Walking, swimming, low-impact aerobics, yoga (modified)

**Activities to avoid**:
- Hot tubs and saunas (hyperthermia risk to fetus)
- Scuba diving (decompression risk)
- Contact sports, horseback riding, skiing, skydiving
- Vigorous exercise during hot, humid weather
- Exercises while lying flat on back (after first trimester)

**Travel during pregnancy**:
- Seatbelt use: Lap belt goes **under the belly**, snug across hips/pelvic bone. Shoulder strap between breasts, off to the side of the belly.
- Take frequent breaks for stretching and bathroom
- Stay hydrated
- Avoid prolonged immobility (increased DVT risk) — compression socks and ROM exercises help

### 10. Common Discomforts of Pregnancy

| Discomfort | Cause | Nursing Interventions |
|-----------|-------|----------------------|
| **Nausea/vomiting** | Rising hCG and hormonal changes | Small, frequent meals; dry crackers before rising; avoid greasy/spicy foods; Vitamin B6 + ginger; avoid strong odors |
| **Heartburn** | Progesterone relaxes esophageal sphincter + uterine pressure | Small, frequent meals; upright position after eating; avoid spicy/fatty foods; antacids if provider-approved |
| **Constipation** | Progesterone slows peristalsis + iron supplements | Increase fluids and fiber; regular exercise; stool softeners if needed |
| **Urinary frequency** | Uterine pressure on bladder (1st and 3rd trimesters) | Normal; report burning or pain (UTI signs); Kegel exercises |
| **Leg cramps** | Pressure on nerves, calcium/phosphorus imbalance | Dorsiflex the foot (point toes up); calcium and magnesium supplementation; avoid pointing toes |
| **Fatigue** | Hormonal changes, increased metabolic demands | Rest periods; balanced nutrition; iron supplementation if anemic |
| **Back pain** | Postural changes, shifting center of gravity | Good body mechanics; pelvic tilt exercises; supportive shoes; avoid high heels |

### Additional Clinical Details from Instructor Review

**Lordosis** — the increased lumbar spinal curve that develops as the center of gravity shifts forward with the growing uterus. This causes the characteristic low back pain of pregnancy. Management includes pelvic tilt exercises and supportive footwear.

**Leukorrhea** — increased vaginal discharge during pregnancy is normal and results from hormonal changes and increased blood flow to the pelvic area.

**Urinary frequency** — normal in both the first trimester (hormonal changes) and third trimester (uterine pressure on the bladder). Report burning or pain, which could indicate UTI.

**Quickening** — first fetal movement felt by the mother at approximately 4-5 months (16-20 weeks). It is classified as a presumptive sign of pregnancy.

**Fundal height landmarks**: At 20 weeks, the fundus reaches the umbilicus. From 20-36 weeks, fundal height in centimeters roughly equals gestational age in weeks. Immediately after delivery, the fundus is at the umbilicus and then descends 1-2 cm per day.

**Smoking in pregnancy** significantly increases the risk of preterm birth, low birth weight, IUGR, and placental complications. Assess tobacco use at every visit and provide cessation resources.

### Key Medications for Chapters 3-5

**Magnesium sulfate**: Used for seizure prevention in preeclampsia and as a tocolytic. Critical nursing point: monitor respiratory rate and hold if RR drops below 12. Also assess deep tendon reflexes (absent DTRs = toxicity) and urine output (must be at least 30 mL/hr). Antidote: calcium gluconate.

**Terbutaline**: Beta-adrenergic tocolytic. Side effects include tachycardia (maternal and fetal), tremors, hyperglycemia, and hypokalemia. Monitor maternal HR, FHR, blood glucose, and potassium.

**Betamethasone**: Corticosteroid for fetal lung maturity. Give 48 hours before anticipated preterm delivery, two doses 24 hours apart.

**Rubella vaccine** (MMR): Live vaccine — NEVER give during pregnancy. If prenatal rubella titer shows non-immunity, vaccinate after delivery. Avoid pregnancy for at least one month after vaccination.

**Amniocentesis**: Performed under ultrasound guidance to avoid the fetus and placenta. Detects genetic disorders and assesses fetal lung maturity. Give RhoGAM afterward in Rh-negative mothers.

**Alpha-fetoprotein (AFP)**: Second trimester screening. Elevated AFP suggests neural tube defects. Low AFP may indicate Down syndrome. Part of the quad screen (AFP, hCG, estriol, inhibin-A).

### 11. Medications and Immunizations in Pregnancy

**FDA Pregnancy Risk Categories**:
- **Category A**: Controlled studies show no risk
- **Category B**: No evidence of risk in humans
- **Category C**: Risk cannot be ruled out
- **Category D**: Positive evidence of risk, but benefits may outweigh
- **Category X**: CONTRAINDICATED — proven fetal abnormalities

**When in doubt, check FDA.gov**. The nurse should always verify pregnancy safety of any medication.

**Immunizations**: Some vaccines are safe during pregnancy (e.g., flu shot, Tdap), while others are contraindicated (live vaccines like MMR, varicella). Rubella vaccine should be given **before** pregnancy if the woman is not immune.

## Chapter 5: Pregnancy Complications (Key Concepts)

### Danger Signs in Pregnancy
These warrant **immediate** notification of the healthcare provider:
- Sudden gush of fluid from vagina (possible ruptured membranes)
- Vaginal bleeding
- Abdominal pain
- Persistent vomiting
- Epigastric pain
- Edema of face and hands
- Severe, persistent headache
- Blurred vision or dizziness
- Chills with fever over 100.4°F
- Painful urination or reduced urine output

### Hyperemesis Gravidarum

This goes beyond normal morning sickness. Hyperemesis gravidarum involves excessive, persistent nausea and vomiting leading to dehydration (which impairs placental perfusion), weight loss greater than 5% of pre-pregnancy weight, electrolyte and acid-base imbalances, and ketonuria. Signs of dehydration include dry tongue and mucous membranes, decreased skin turgor, scant concentrated urine, and elevated hematocrit. Treatment involves IV fluid resuscitation, electrolyte replacement, antiemetics, and small frequent meals once tolerated.

### Types of Miscarriage (Abortion)

Understanding the different types is important for nursing assessment and communication:

- **Threatened**: Vaginal bleeding but cervix remains closed. Pregnancy may continue. Nursing care includes bed rest and monitoring.
- **Inevitable**: Cervix is dilating. Pregnancy cannot be saved. Bleeding and cramping increase.
- **Incomplete**: Some products of conception expelled but tissue remains. May require D&C.
- **Complete**: All products of conception expelled spontaneously. Bleeding subsides.
- **Missed**: Fetus dies but is retained in the uterus. May require medical or surgical intervention.

Post-miscarriage teaching: report increased bleeding, no tampons (infection risk), take temperature every 8 hours for 3 days, report fever 100.4 or above or foul-smelling discharge, avoid sexual activity until cleared. Use the term "miscarriage" rather than "abortion" with families. Provide emotional support, offer mementos if appropriate, chaplain referral, and support group information.

### Bleeding Disorders

**Ectopic pregnancy**: Implantation outside the uterus, most commonly in the fallopian tube. If tube ruptures: sudden severe lower abdominal pain, vaginal bleeding, signs of hypovolemic shock, and possible shoulder pain (from blood irritating the diaphragm). Treatment: observation if very early, methotrexate (inhibits cell division), or surgical removal. Priority is always to control bleeding.

**Hydatidiform mole** (molar pregnancy): Abnormal trophoblastic growth creating sac-like vesicles resembling tiny grapes. Signs: vaginal bleeding, rapid uterine growth, no fetal heart activity, hyperemesis, unusually early preeclampsia, very high hCG, and "snowstorm" ultrasound pattern. Treatment is uterine evacuation. hCG must be followed until undetectable and monitored for at least 1 year due to choriocarcinoma risk.

**Placenta previa vs. placental abruption**:
| Feature | Placenta Previa | Placental Abruption |
|---------|----------------|-------------------|
| Location | Placenta implants low, covering cervix | Placenta separates from uterine wall |
| Bleeding | **Painless**, bright red | **Painful**, dark red (may be concealed) |
| Uterus | Soft, relaxed | Firm, board-like, tender |
| Onset | Gradual | Can be sudden |
| Risk factors | Prior C-sections, multiparity | Cocaine use, HTN, preeclampsia, trauma |
| Key nursing point | No vaginal exams | Monitor for DIC |

### Hypertensive Disorders of Pregnancy

**Preeclampsia** (after 20 weeks gestation):
- Classic triad: **Hypertension + Proteinuria + Edema**
- Risk factors: first pregnancy (G1), obesity, family history, age <19 or >35, multiples, chronic HTN, DM, renal disease, autoimmune disease
- Labs: hemoglobin, hematocrit, platelets, urine protein, serum creatinine, uric acid, liver transaminases
- Treatment: magnesium sulfate (seizure prevention), labetalol (antihypertensive of choice), activity restriction, frequent fetal evaluation
- **Birth is the cure for preeclampsia**

**HELLP syndrome** — a severe form often seen in preeclamptic patients:
- **H**emolysis
- **E**levated **L**iver enzymes
- **L**ow **P**latelets
- Signs: RUQ pain, epigastric pain, nausea, vomiting, malaise
- Usually requires ICU care, magnesium sulfate, and expedient delivery

**Eclampsia**: Progression to seizures. Medical emergency.

### Gestational Diabetes Mellitus (GDM)
- Glucose intolerance with onset during pregnancy
- Screening: glucose challenge test at **24-28 weeks**
- Maternal effects of pregnancy hormones: increased insulin resistance and faster insulin breakdown
- Maternal risks: spontaneous abortion, preeclampsia, preterm labor, polyhydramnios, infections, macrosomia complications, ketoacidosis
- Fetal risks: congenital abnormalities, macrosomia (large baby), birth injury, delayed lung maturation, RDS, neonatal hypoglycemia, hypocalcemia, hyperbilirubinemia, polycythemia
- Treatment: diet, exercise, blood glucose monitoring, insulin if needed
- In true GDM, glucose usually returns to normal by **6 weeks postpartum**

### Rh Incompatibility
When an **Rh-negative mother** carries an **Rh-positive fetus**, fetal blood cells can enter the maternal circulation (especially at delivery). The mother's immune system creates antibodies against the Rh factor. In subsequent pregnancies with Rh+ fetuses, these antibodies cross the placenta and destroy fetal red blood cells (**erythroblastosis fetalis**).

Prevention: **RhoGAM (Rh immunoglobulin)**
- Given at **28 weeks** gestation
- Given within **72 hours after delivery** of an Rh+ infant
- Also given after any event where fetal-maternal blood mixing could occur (miscarriage, amniocentesis, trauma)

### TORCH Infections
These infections are particularly dangerous during pregnancy:

| | Infection | Transmission/Cause | Effects on Fetus/Newborn |
|---|---------|-------------------|------------------------|
| **T** | Toxoplasmosis | Cat feces, raw/undercooked meat | Low birth weight, enlarged liver/spleen, jaundice, anemia, eye inflammation, neurologic damage |
| **O** | Other (Syphilis, Hep B, GBS, etc.) | Various; GBS diagnosed by vaginal/rectal culture at 35-37 weeks | GBS: sepsis, pneumonia, meningitis in newborn; prevented with IV antibiotics during labor |
| **R** | Rubella | Viral; vaccine should be given BEFORE pregnancy | Microcephaly, intellectual disability, congenital cataracts, deafness, cardiac defects, IUGR |
| **C** | Cytomegalovirus (CMV) | Most common congenital viral infection | Varies from asymptomatic to severe disability |
| **H** | Herpes simplex | Direct contact; active lesions at delivery → C-section indicated | Neonatal herpes (potentially fatal) |

### Other Key Conditions

**Sickle cell disease in pregnancy**: Autosomal recessive disorder. Pregnancy can trigger a sickle cell crisis with massive RBC destruction and vessel occlusion. Risk to fetus: placental vessel occlusion leading to preterm birth, growth restriction, fetal demise. Oxygen and fluids given continuously throughout labor.

**Iron-deficiency anemia**: Most common nutritional anemia in pregnancy. Symptoms: fatigue, pale skin/mucous membranes, SOB, tachycardia. Treatment: oral iron supplementation; continue 3 months after correction.

**Folic acid deficiency anemia**: Treatment with 1 mg/day folic acid (higher dose if history of neural tube defect in previous child).

**DIC (Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation)**: Life-threatening emergency where clot formation and destruction co-occur, depleting clotting factors. Signs: bleeding from nose, gums, incisions, venipuncture sites; increasing uterine size (blood accumulation); tender, hard uterus. Treatment: clotting factors and blood products.

### Hypovolemic Shock

Hypovolemic shock is the major risk of hemorrhage during pregnancy. It interrupts blood flow to body cells, preventing oxygenation and nutrient delivery. The body initially compensates by increasing heart rate and respiratory rate to speed circulation of remaining blood. As shock progresses, blood flow to non-essential organs decreases and eventually stops.

Signs and symptoms in order of progression: fetal heart rate changes (earliest fetal sign), rising weak pulse (tachycardia — the earliest maternal sign), rising respiratory rate with shallow irregular respirations, falling blood pressure (a late sign), decreased or absent urinary output (less than 30 mL/hr), pale cold clammy skin, faintness, and thirst. Nursing interventions focus on fluid resuscitation, blood replacement, and treating the underlying cause.

### Heart Disease in Pregnancy

The dramatic cardiovascular changes of pregnancy (40-50% blood volume increase, elevated clotting factors) can overwhelm a compromised heart. If the heart cannot handle the increased workload, congestive heart failure results, and the fetus suffers from reduced placental blood flow. Increased clotting factors also raise the risk of thrombosis. Signs of CHF during pregnancy must be monitored carefully and managed with the obstetric and cardiology teams collaboratively.

### Anemias in Pregnancy

**Iron-deficiency anemia** is the most common nutritional anemia in pregnancy. Symptoms include easy fatigue, pale skin and mucous membranes, shortness of breath, pounding heart, and rapid pulse with severe anemia. Treatment involves oral iron supplementation, which should continue for approximately 3 months after the anemia has been corrected. Iron should be taken with vitamin C for better absorption.

**Folic acid deficiency anemia** is treated with 1 mg/day of folic acid — over twice the preventive supplement dose. Women who have had a previous child with a neural tube defect may need an even higher dose.

**Sickle cell disease** is an autosomal recessive disorder where abnormal hemoglobin causes red blood cells to become sickle-shaped during hypoxic or acidotic episodes. Pregnancy can trigger a sickle cell crisis with massive red blood cell destruction and vessel occlusion. Risk to the fetus includes placental vessel occlusion leading to preterm birth, growth restriction, and fetal demise. Oxygen and fluids should be given continuously throughout labor. Patients must be taught to prevent dehydration and avoid activities that cause hypoxia.

### Urinary Tract Infections

UTIs are more common during pregnancy due to hormonal changes that cause urinary stasis. Simple UTIs can progress to pyelonephritis, which presents with high fever, chills, flank pain or tenderness, nausea, and vomiting. Pyelonephritis is serious because it can trigger preterm labor. Treatment includes antibiotics (full course must be completed), increased fluid intake, and monitoring for preterm contractions.

### Environmental Hazards and Substance Use

Multiple substances are harmful to the developing fetus: alcohol (fetal alcohol spectrum disorders), cocaine (placental abruption, preterm birth), marijuana, tobacco (IUGR, low birth weight), heroin (neonatal abstinence syndrome), amphetamines, and certain anticoagulants. The nurse should assess for substance use in a nonjudgmental manner during prenatal care and provide education about the risks to fetal development.
