## Chapter 6: Nursing Care of Mother and Infant During Labor and Birth

### 1. Settings for Childbirth

**Hospital Birth**
- Advantages: Immediate access to emergency care, pain relief options, NICU available
- Disadvantages: May feel less personal, higher intervention rates, less control over environment

**Freestanding Birth Centers**
- Advantages: Home-like setting, lower intervention rates, certified nurse-midwives
- Disadvantages: Must transfer to hospital for complications, limited pain relief options

**Home Birth**
- Advantages: Familiar environment, family-centered, lower costs
- Disadvantages: No immediate access to surgical/emergency care, limited pain relief

### 2. The Four "P's" of the Birth Process

**Powers** — Uterine contractions and maternal pushing effort
- Primary power: involuntary uterine contractions that cause cervical effacement and dilation
- Secondary power: voluntary bearing-down effort by the mother

**Passage** — The birth canal
- Bony pelvis (true pelvis with inlet, midpelvis, outlet)
- Soft tissues (cervix, vagina, pelvic floor)

**Passenger** — The fetus
- Size, presentation (part entering pelvis first), position (relationship to maternal pelvis), lie (longitudinal vs. transverse)

**Psyche** — Mental and emotional state
- Anxiety, fear, cultural beliefs, support system all influence labor progress

### 3. Additional Factors Affecting Labor (6 P's)

Beyond the four P's, labor is influenced by:
1. **Preparation** — Prenatal education and birth planning
2. **Position** — Maternal positioning during labor
3. **Professional** — Skills and approach of the birth attendant
4. **Place** — Physical environment of birth
5. **Procedures** — Interventions performed
6. **People** — Support persons present

### 4. Uterine Contractions

**Effect on the Cervix**:
- **Effacement**: Thinning and shortening of the cervical canal from 0% (thick) to 100% (paper-thin)
- **Dilation**: Opening of the cervical os from 0 cm to 10 cm (complete)

**Phases of a Contraction**:
1. **Increment** — Contraction builds in intensity
2. **Peak (Acme)** — Strongest point of contraction
3. **Decrement** — Contraction subsides

**Assessment Terms**:
- **Frequency**: Time from start of one contraction to start of the next (e.g., every 3 minutes)
- **Duration**: Length of one contraction (e.g., 60 seconds)
- **Intensity**: Strength — mild, moderate, or firm (assessed by palpation or IUPC)

### 5. Signs of Impending Labor

- **Braxton Hicks contractions**: Irregular, painless "practice" contractions that become more regular near term
- **Increased vaginal discharge**: Clear or slightly bloody
- **Bloody show**: Mucus plug expelled with small amount of blood — reliable sign
- **Rupture of membranes**: May be a gush or trickle of amniotic fluid
- **Energy spurt ("nesting")**: Sudden burst of energy to prepare home/baby area
- **Weight loss**: 1-3 lbs from fluid shifts
- **Lightening/dropping**: Baby descends into pelvis; mother may breathe easier but have more bladder pressure
- **Loose bowel movements**: Muscles relax in preparation

### 6. True vs. False Labor

| Feature | True Labor | False Labor |
|---------|------------|-------------|
| **Contractions** | Regular pattern, ↑ frequency and intensity | Irregular, no progressive pattern |
| **Cervical change** | Progressive effacement and dilation | No change |
| **Effect of position change** | Contractions continue | May stop or decrease |
| **Pain location** | Starts in back, radiates to abdomen | May be felt in abdomen only |
| **Walking** | Intensifies contractions | May relieve discomfort |

### 7. When to Go to the Hospital

**3-2-1 Rule** (especially for first-time mothers):
- Contractions are **3 minutes apart**
- Lasting **1 minute (60 seconds)** each
- Continuing for at least **1 hour**

Other indications:
- Ruptured membranes
- Vaginal bleeding (other than bloody show)
- Decreased or absent fetal movement
- Any other concern

### 8. Mechanisms (Cardinal Movements) of Labor

These are the positional changes the fetal head makes as it navigates the birth canal:

1. **Descent** — Baby moves down through the pelvis
2. **Engagement** — Widest diameter of fetal head enters pelvic inlet
3. **Flexion** — Chin tucks to chest (smallest diameter presents)
4. **Internal Rotation** — Head rotates to align with pelvic outlet
5. **Extension** — Head extends as it passes under pubis
6. **External Rotation (Restitution)** — Head rotates back to align with shoulders
7. **Expulsion** — Shoulders and body deliver

### 9. Stages and Phases of Labor

**First Stage: Dilation and Effacement** (longest stage — 4-6 hours average)
- **Latent phase**: 0-3 cm dilation; contractions mild, irregular
- **Active phase**: 4-7 cm dilation; contractions stronger, more frequent
- **Transition phase**: 8-10 cm dilation; most intense, shortest

**Second Stage: Expulsion** (30 minutes to 2 hours)
- Full dilation to birth of baby
- Mother actively pushes with contractions

**Third Stage: Placental Expulsion** (5-30 minutes)
- Placenta separates from uterine wall and delivers
- **Schultze mechanism**: Placenta delivers "shiny side" (fetal surface) first
- **Duncan mechanism**: Placenta delivers "dirty side" (maternal surface) first

**Fourth Stage: Recovery** (1-2 hours postpartum)
- Critical period for hemorrhage prevention
- Uterus must contract firmly to clamp blood vessels
- Bonding and initial newborn care occur

### 10. Admission to the Labor Unit

**Three Major Assessments Performed Promptly**:
1. **Fetal condition** — FHR, amniotic fluid status
2. **Maternal condition** — VS, contractions, progress of labor
3. **Impending birth** — Is delivery imminent?

**Admission Procedures**:
- Permits and consents
- Laboratory tests (CBC, type/Rh, urinalysis)
- IV placement
- Perineal prep (if needed)
- Determining fetal position and presentation (Leopold maneuvers, vaginal exam)

### 11. Fetal Heart Rate Monitoring

**Baseline FHR**: 110-160 beats/min

**Monitoring Frequency**:
- **Low-risk, active first stage**: Every 30 minutes
- **Second stage**: Every 15 minutes, preferably before each contraction
- **With risk factors**: Every 15 minutes (first stage), every 5 minutes (second stage)

**Methods**:
- **Intermittent auscultation**: Listening with Doppler or fetoscope
- **Continuous electronic fetal monitoring (EFM)**: External (ultrasound transducer + toco) or internal (fetal scalp electrode + IUPC)

### 12. FHR Patterns — Reassuring vs. Nonreassuring

**Reassuring Patterns**:
- Stable baseline FHR within normal range
- **Moderate variability** (fluctuations of 6-25 bpm)
- **Accelerations** (↑ 15 bpm × 15 sec — "reassuring")
- Contractions >2 min apart with ≥60 sec relaxation between

**Nonreassuring Patterns**:
- Tachycardia or bradycardia
- Decreased or absent variability
- **Late decelerations** (begin after contraction peak — placental insufficiency)
- **Variable decelerations** (abrupt drops — cord compression)

### 13. VEAL CHOP — Deceleration Mnemonic ⭐

| Letter | Deceleration | Cause | Intervention |
|--------|--------------|-------|--------------|
| **V** | Variable | **C**ord compression | Reposition mother, O2, amnioinfusion |
| **E** | Early | **H**ead compression | None (normal finding) |
| **A** | Acceleration | **O**K! | None (reassuring) |
| **L** | Late | **P**lacental insufficiency | O2, left lateral position, stop Pitocin, notify provider |

### 14. Amniotic Fluid Assessment

**Color**:
- Clear with white flecks of vernix = normal
- **Green or meconium-stained** = fetus passed stool; possible fetal compromise, risk of meconium aspiration syndrome (MAS)

**Odor**:
- Should be odorless
- Foul odor suggests infection

**Amount**:
- Scant: trickle
- Moderate: ~500 mL
- Large: ≥1000 mL

### 15. Apgar Scoring

Performed at **1 minute** and **5 minutes** after birth:

| Component | 0 | 1 | 2 |
|-----------|---|---|---|
| **Heart rate** | Absent | <100 bpm | ≥100 bpm |
| **Respiratory effort** | Absent | Slow, irregular | Good cry |
| **Muscle tone** | Flaccid | Some flexion | Active motion |
| **Reflex irritability** | None | Grimace | Cry or cough |
| **Color** | Blue/pale all over | Body pink, extremities blue | Completely pink |

**Interpretation**:
- 7-10: Normal
- 4-6: Moderate depression; may need stimulation and oxygen
- 0-3: Severe depression; needs aggressive resuscitation

### 16. Immediate Newborn Care (Phase 1: Birth to 1 Hour)

- **Maintain thermoregulation**: Dry baby, place skin-to-skin, warm blankets, radiant warmer
- **Maintain cardiorespiratory function**: Suction airway if needed, assess respirations
- **Observe for urination and passage of meconium**
- **Identify mother, father, and newborn** (bracelets)
- **Brief assessment for major anomalies**
- **Encourage bonding and breastfeeding** (golden hour)

### 17. Newborn Medications

**Vitamin K (AquaMEPHYTON)**:
- Given IM in vastus lateralis
- Prevents hemorrhagic disease of the newborn (vitamin K deficiency bleeding)

**Erythromycin Eye Ointment**:
- Applied to both eyes
- Prevents ophthalmia neonatorum (gonococcal/chlamydial conjunctivitis)

### 18. VBAC (Vaginal Birth After Cesarean)

**Main concern**: Uterine scar rupture
- Can disrupt placental blood flow
- Lead to hemorrhage

**Nursing care**:
- Woman may need more support than other laboring women
- Provide empathy and encouragement
- Monitor for signs of rupture (sudden pain, FHR changes, hemorrhage)

### 19. Immediate Postpartum Period (Third and Fourth Stages)

**Third Stage (placental delivery)**:
- Observe for signs of placental separation (gush of blood, lengthening cord, fundus rises)
- Schultze or Duncan presentation

**Fourth Stage Nursing Care**:
- Identify and prevent hemorrhage (fundal massage, assess lochia)
- Evaluate and intervene for pain
- Observe bladder function and urine output
- Evaluate recovery from anesthesia
- Provide initial care to newborn
- Promote bonding and attachment

## Chapter 7: Nursing Management of Pain During Labor and Birth

### 1. Childbirth and Pain — How It Differs

Unlike other types of pain:
- Part of a **normal birth process**
- Woman has **months to prepare** for pain management
- **Self-limiting** — rapidly declines after birth
- Associated with a **positive outcome** (baby!)

### 2. Factors That Influence Labor Pain

**Pain Threshold**: Individual variation in how pain is perceived

**Sources of Pain**:
- Cervical dilation and effacement
- Uterine muscle ischemia (reduced blood flow during contractions)
- Pressure on bladder, urethra, bowel
- Stretching of birth canal

**CNS Factors**:
- **Gate control theory**: Non-painful stimuli (massage, pressure) can "close the gate" to pain signals
- **Endorphins**: Natural pain-relieving hormones released during labor

**Maternal Conditions**:
- Cervical readiness (effaced, anterior = less painful)
- Pelvis shape and size
- Labor intensity and rapidity
- Fatigue level
- Fetal presentation and position (OP = more back pain)

### 3. Childbirth Preparation Methods

**Dick-Read Method**:
- Fear → Tension → Pain cycle
- Education and relaxation break this cycle
- "Childbirth Without Fear"

**Bradley Method**:
- Husband-coached natural childbirth
- Focus on nutrition, exercise, relaxation
- Minimal intervention philosophy

**Lamaze Method**:
- Breathing techniques, focal points, relaxation
- Partner support
- Knowledge reduces fear

### 4. Nonpharmacologic Pain Relief Measures

**Advantages**:
- No harm to mother or fetus
- Do not slow labor if providing adequate pain control
- No allergy or adverse drug effects

**Techniques**:
- **Effleurage**: Light, rhythmic stroking of the abdomen
- **Breathing**: Slow, patterned, or accelerated-paced
- Position changes
- Hydrotherapy (shower, tub)
- Massage and counterpressure
- Heat/cold application
- Visualization and imagery
- Music and aromatherapy
- **Doula**: Continuous labor support

**Hyperventilation Recognition and Correction**:
- Signs: Dizziness, tingling hands/feet, muscle cramps, numbness around nose/mouth, blurred vision
- Correction: Breathe slowly, especially exhaling; breathe into cupped hands; place moist washcloth over nose/mouth; hold breath briefly before exhaling

### 5. Pharmacologic Pain Management

**Categories**:
- **Analgesics**: Relieve pain (systemic or regional)
- **Anesthetics**: Block sensation (regional or general)

### 6. Systemic Analgesics

**Narcotic (Opioid) Analgesics**:
- Provide pain relief without loss of consciousness
- Examples: fentanyl, morphine, nalbuphine
- **Avoid if birth anticipated within 1 hour** (neonatal respiratory depression)

**Narcotic Antagonist**:
- **Naloxone (Narcan)**: Reverses respiratory depression in mother and newborn

**Adjunctive Drugs**:
- Relieve nausea and vomiting associated with opioids
- Examples: promethazine, hydroxyzine

### 7. Regional Anesthesia

Involves placing anesthetic in epidural or subarachnoid space around spinal cord.

**Meninges (from outer to inner)**:
1. Dura mater
2. Arachnoid mater
3. Pia mater

**Difference Between Analgesic and Anesthetic**:
- **Analgesic**: Blocks pain sensation
- **Anesthetic**: Blocks both pain and motor responses

### 8. Types of Regional Anesthesia for Childbirth

| Type | Where Given | Effect | Notes |
|------|-------------|--------|-------|
| **Local infiltration** | Perineum | Numbs small area | For episiotomy/repair |
| **Pudendal block** | Pudendal nerve (ischial spine) | Perineum numbed | Vaginal birth, forceps, vacuum |
| **Epidural block** | Epidural space (T10-S5) | Pain relief; may retain motor | Continuous infusion; most common |
| **Spinal (subarachnoid) block** | Subarachnoid space | Complete pain/motor block below level | Single dose; rapid onset; common for C-section |

### 9. General Anesthesia

**When Used**:
- Emergency cesarean when not enough time for block
- Cesarean in woman who refuses or has contraindication to regional

**Adverse Effects — Mother**:
- **Regurgitation with aspiration** of gastric contents → chemical lung injury
- Respiratory depression

**Adverse Effects — Newborn**:
- **Respiratory depression**
- Aggressive resuscitation may be necessary

### 10. Pregnancy Physiology and Anesthesia

- **↑ Hypoxia risk**: Reduced FRC, ↑ O2 consumption
- **Sluggish GI tract**: ↑ Risk of vomiting and aspiration
- **Aortocaval compression**: Uterus compresses IVC when supine → hypotension; **use left lateral tilt**
- **Effect on fetus**: All medications cross placenta to some degree

### 11. Nurse's Role in Pharmacologic Pain Management

- Begins at admission: assess woman's preference for pain relief
- Keep side rails up (sedation risk)
- Provide education regarding procedures and expected effects
- **Observe for hypotension** (especially after epidural/spinal)
- **Observe for respiratory depression**
- Document interventions and assessments

## Chapter 8: Nursing Care of Women with Complications During Labor and Birth

### 1. Induction vs. Augmentation of Labor

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Induction** | Intentional initiation of labor before it occurs naturally |
| **Augmentation** | Stimulation of contractions after they have started naturally |

### 2. Bishop Score ⭐

A tool to assess cervical readiness for induction. A score **above 6** indicates the cervix is favorable for induction.

**Components scored**:
- Dilation (0-3 cm)
- Effacement (0-80%)
- Station (-3 to +3)
- Cervical consistency (firm, medium, soft)
- Cervical position (posterior, mid, anterior)

### 3. Indications for Labor Induction

- Post-term pregnancy
- PROM (premature rupture of membranes)
- Maternal medical conditions (preeclampsia, diabetes, cardiac disease)
- Fetal compromise
- Fetal demise
- Chorioamnionitis

### 4. Contraindications to Labor Induction

- **Cephalopelvic disproportion (CPD)**
- **Placenta previa**
- **Active herpes infection**
- Fetal malpresentation (breech, transverse)
- Umbilical cord prolapsed
- Previous classical cesarean incision
- Active genital herpes

### 5. Pharmacologic Methods to Stimulate Contractions

**Oxytocin (Pitocin)**:
- Most common agent for induction/augmentation
- Must be titrated carefully
- Nurse monitors contractions and FHR closely

**Prostaglandins**:
- **Cervidil (dinoprostone/PGE2)**: Vaginal insert for cervical ripening
- **Cytotec (misoprostol/PGE1)**: Off-label use for cervical ripening

### 6. Cervical Dilation Methods

**Mechanical**:
- **Laminaria**: Seaweed sticks that absorb moisture and expand
- **Transcervical balloon dilators**: Balloon catheter inserted through cervical os and inflated

**Pharmacologic**:
- Prostaglandin preparations

### 7. Amniotomy (AROM)

**Artificial Rupture of Membranes**
- Performed to stimulate or enhance contractions
- **Commits the woman to delivery** (cannot delay birth)

**Complications**:
- **Prolapse of the umbilical cord**
- **Infection**
- **Abruptio placentae**

**Nursing care**: Monitor FHR immediately after; observe fluid characteristics

### 8. Amnioinfusion

**Definition**: Infusion of warmed sterile saline or LR into the uterus via IUPC after membranes have ruptured

**Indications**:
- **Oligohydramnios** (too little fluid)
- **Umbilical cord compression** causing variable decelerations
- **Diluting meconium-stained amniotic fluid** to prevent MAS

### 9. Version (External Cephalic Version)

**Definition**: Manually turning a breech or transverse fetus to cephalic presentation

**Performed**: Usually 37+ weeks if vaginal delivery desired

**Contraindications**: Placenta previa, nonreassuring FHR, uterine anomalies, oligohydramnios

### 10. Perineal Lacerations

**Uncontrolled tearing of perineal tissue**:

| Degree | Structures Involved |
|--------|---------------------|
| **First** | Superficial vaginal mucosa or perineal skin |
| **Second** | Vaginal mucosa, perineal skin, and deeper tissues |
| **Third** | Same as second + anal sphincter |
| **Fourth** | Extends through anal sphincter into rectal mucosa |

**Nursing care**: Comfort measures (ice, analgesics), wound assessment, sitz baths, stool softeners

### 11. Episiotomy

**Definition**: Surgical incision of the perineum to enlarge the vaginal opening

**Types**: Midline (vertical) or mediolateral

**Current practice**: Generally avoided unless medically necessary; spontaneous tears heal better

### 12. Forceps and Vacuum Extraction

**Indications**: Prolonged second stage, maternal exhaustion, fetal distress, maternal conditions that prohibit pushing

**Risks**:
- **Maternal**: Lacerations, hematomas, hemorrhage
- **Fetal**: Bruising, facial/scalp lacerations, cephalohematoma, chignon (vacuum), retinal hemorrhage

### 13. Cesarean Birth

**Indications**:
- CPD
- Breech or other malpresentation
- Placenta previa or abruption
- Fetal distress
- Failed induction or labor progress
- Previous C-section (depending on type)
- Maternal conditions (herpes, HIV, cardiac disease)

**Incision Types**:

| Skin Incision | Uterine Incision | VBAC Possible? |
|---------------|------------------|----------------|
| Transverse (Pfannenstiel) | Low transverse | ✅ Yes |
| Vertical | Low vertical | ⚠️ Riskier |
| — | **Classic** | ❌ No (highest rupture risk) |

**Risks to Mother**:
- Anesthesia complications
- Hemorrhage
- Blood clots (DVT/PE)
- Injury to urinary tract
- Delayed peristalsis (paralytic ileus)
- Infection

**Risks to Newborn**:
- Inadvertent preterm birth
- Respiratory problems (TTN, RDS)
- Injury (bruising, laceration)

### 14. Dystocia (Difficult Labor)

**Definition**: Abnormal labor caused by abnormality of the "four P's"

**Power (Contractions)**:
- Hypotonic: Weak, ineffective contractions
- Hypertonic: Excessive, uncoordinated contractions

**Passenger (Fetus)**:
- Size (macrosomia)
- Presentation (breech, face, brow)
- Position (OP — "sunny side up")
- Multifetal pregnancy
- Birth defects

**Passage (Pelvis/Soft Tissues)**:
- CPD
- Contracted pelvis
- Soft tissue resistance

**Psyche (Psychological)**:
- Lack of analgesic control of excessive pain
- **Absence of a support person or coach**
- **Immobility and restriction to bed**
- **Inability to carry out cultural traditions**
- **Anxiety causes hormone release that affects labor**

### 15. Nursing Care for Abnormal Fetal Presentation/Position

Encourage positions that favor fetal rotation and descent and reduce back pain:
- Sitting, kneeling, or standing while leaning forward
- Rocking pelvis back and forth on hands and knees
- **Side-lying**
- **Squatting** (second stage)
- Lunging (one foot on chair, knee/foot pointed to that side)

### 16. Multifetal Pregnancy

**Challenges**:
- Preterm labor risk
- Malpresentation of one or more fetuses
- Cord entanglement
- Uterine overdistention → postpartum hemorrhage

**Delivery**: Often requires C-section if first twin is not cephalic

### 17. Preterm Labor ⭐

**Risk Factors**:
- Previous preterm birth
- Exposure to DES
- Underweight
- Chronic illness
- Dehydration
- Preeclampsia
- Previous pregnancy losses
- **Substance abuse**
- **Chronic stress**
- **Infection**
- **Anemia**
- Preterm PROM
- **Inadequate prenatal care**
- Poor nutrition
- Low education level
- **Poverty**
- **Smoking**
- **Multifetal pregnancy**

**Maternal Symptoms**:
- Contractions (uncomfortable or painless)
- "Balling up" sensation
- Menstrual-like cramps
- **Constant low backache**
- Pelvic pressure
- Change in vaginal discharge
- Abdominal cramps ± diarrhea
- Vulva/thigh discomfort
- "Just feeling bad" or "coming down with something"

### 18. Fetal Fibronectin (fFN) Test

**What it is**: A protein produced by fetal membranes

**When tested**: 22-24 weeks

**Interpretation**:
- Present in vaginal secretions → predicts increased risk of PTL
- Absent → low risk of PTL in next 1-2 weeks

**Mechanism**: fFN helps keep amniotic sac attached to uterine lining; leaks into vaginal secretions if uterine activity, infection, or cervical effacement occurs

### 19. Diagnosis of Preterm Labor

- Cervical effacement and dilation of **more than 2 cm**
- Shortened cervical length on ultrasound at 20 weeks (predictive)

### 20. Tocolytic Therapy

**Tocolytics**: Medications used to **stop or delay premature labor contractions**

**Contraindications**:
- Preeclampsia
- Placenta previa
- Abruptio placentae
- Gestational age over 37 weeks
- Chorioamnionitis
- Fetal demise

**Common Tocolytics**:

| Drug | Mechanism | Key Nursing Points |
|------|-----------|-------------------|
| **Magnesium sulfate** | CNS depressant; relaxes smooth muscle | Monitor RR (>12), DTRs (absent = toxicity), urine output (>30 mL/hr); antidote = **calcium gluconate** |
| **Terbutaline (Brethine)** | Beta-2 adrenergic agonist | Tachycardia, tremors, hyperglycemia, hypokalemia |
| **Nifedipine (Procardia)** | Calcium channel blocker | ↓ BP; monitor HR |

**Nursing Care**:
- Hydration
- Check cultures (bacteria, GBS, possible SROM)
- Fetal fibronectin
- If PTB inevitable: give steroids for lung maturity

### 21. Betamethasone for Fetal Lung Maturity

**Purpose**: Accelerate fetal lung maturity before anticipated preterm delivery

**Dosing**: 2 doses, **24 hours apart**, ideally **48 hours before** delivery

**Alternative**: Dexamethasone (if betamethasone not available)

### 22. Prolonged Pregnancy

**Definition**: Pregnancy lasting longer than 40 weeks

**Testing to confirm/monitor**:
- **NST** (nonstress test)
- **AFI** (amniotic fluid index)
- **BPP** (biophysical profile) — score 8-10 normal, ≤6 concerning
- **Kick counts**

### 23. Obstetric Emergencies

**Prolapsed Umbilical Cord**
- Cord slips between presenting part and birth canal
- **Types**: Complete (visible), Palpated (felt on exam), Occult (hidden, suspected by FHR)

**Immediate Nursing Actions**:
1. **Call for help immediately**
2. **Knee-chest position** (or Trendelenburg/side-lying with hips elevated)
3. **Insert gloved hand into vagina and push presenting part OFF cord**
4. **Do not remove hand** until baby is delivered
5. May need to ride on stretcher to OR and stay in position during C-section
6. Administer **terbutaline** to stop contractions

**Uterine Rupture**
- **Complete**: Through all uterine layers
- **Incomplete**: Through some layers
- **Dehiscence**: Separation of old scar

**Risk factors**: Prior C-section, trauma, hyperstimulation with oxytocin

**Signs**: Sudden severe pain, hemorrhage, loss of contractions, fetal distress, maternal shock

**Treatment**: Immediate C-section, blood products, possible hysterectomy

**Amniotic Fluid Embolism (Anaphylactoid Syndrome)**
- Amniotic fluid enters maternal circulation

**Signs**: Sudden respiratory distress, cyanosis, cardiovascular collapse, DIC, seizures, coma

**Treatment**:
- Respiratory support (intubation, ventilation)
- Treat shock with electrolytes and volume expanders
- Replace coagulation factors (platelets, fibrinogen)
- Monitor I/O, pulse ox
- Likely ICU transfer
